# C4 evidence pack — six D1 theory, framework, and research-tradition pages

> Date: 2026-07-13  
> Scope: research support for the six existing C4 D1 entries only. This is not publishable page copy and does not add theories, disciplines, works, or concepts.  
> Evidence labels: **L1** = directly traceable source or bibliographic fact; **L2** = explicitly editorial synthesis, comparison, or fit judgement; **L3** = context-dependent research-design option, not a universal methodological or causal conclusion.

## Cross-page editorial rule

The six labels do **not** name six homogeneous, equally bounded theories. C4 must retain the following distinctions in the page definition and verification language:

| Existing page label | Accurate page-level nature | Do not present it as |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Teacher Professional Development Theory | a plural field of teacher professional-development / professional-learning theories and models; this D1 page is an editorial entry lens | one canonical, closed theory or evidence that any activity causes improved practice/outcomes |
| Teacher Life History Research | a qualitative research tradition / methodological approach to biographical and narrative inquiry | a causal theory, transparent access to factual biography, or population-estimation method |
| Educational Equity Theory | an editorial umbrella for normative and empirical equity/inclusion inquiry | one classic theory with a single founder, universal equity criterion, or a neutral descriptive label |
| Institutional Theory | a family of institutionalisms; the existing source anchors the organisational neo-institutionalist strand | an account in which all organisations passively comply or a synonym for any “institution” |
| Street-Level Bureaucracy | a public-policy/administration theoretical perspective on frontline implementation | a theory of all workers, an agenda-setting framework, or evidence that discretion is necessarily good/bad |
| Multiple Streams Framework | a policy-process analytical framework / perspective | a universal causal law, a generic three-factor metaphor, or a complete implementation theory |

## A. Teacher Professional Development Theory

### A1. Source register and source-backed claims

1. Christopher Day, *Developing Teachers: The Challenges of Lifelong Learning* (1999), [Routledge publisher record](https://www.routledge.com/Developing-Teachers-The-Challenges-of-Lifelong-Learning/Day/p/book/9780750707480). **L1:** authorised bibliographic anchor for continuing teacher development and lifelong-learning framing.
2. Thomas R. Guskey, “Professional Development and Teacher Change” (2002), *Teachers and Teaching*, 8(3), 381–391, [DOI record](https://doi.org/10.1080/135406002100000512). **L1:** a theory/model of teacher change that treats professional development, changed classroom practices, student learning outcomes, and teacher beliefs/attitudes as related but distinct components; use it to avoid reducing development to attendance.
3. David Clarke and Hilary Hollingsworth, “Elaborating a Model of Teacher Professional Growth” (2002), *Teaching and Teacher Education*, 18(8), 947–967, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0742-051X(02)00053-7). **L1:** proposes a non-linear professional-growth model with external, personal, practice, and salient-outcome domains, joined by mediating processes; it is one model, not a universal causal law for all professional development.
4. Laura M. Desimone, “Improving Impact Studies of Teachers’ Professional Development” (2009), *Educational Researcher*, 38(3), 181–199, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.3102/0013189X08331140). **L1:** proposes a common conceptual framework for impact research and warns against treating any one data method as automatically privileged; it is not a singular theory of all teacher development.
5. Helen Timperley, Aaron Wilson, Heather Barrar, and Irene Fung, *Teacher Professional Learning and Development: Best Evidence Synthesis Iteration* (2007), [New Zealand Ministry of Education record](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/2515/15341). **L1:** an authoritative evidence-synthesis record that distinguishes professional learning conditions, learning processes, teacher knowledge/skills, and student outcomes; it is not evidence that one activity works in every setting.
6. Christopher Day, Amanda Kington, Gordon Stobart, and Pam Sammons, “The Personal and Professional Selves of Teachers” (2006), *British Educational Research Journal*, 32(4), 601–616, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/01411920600775316). **L1:** a bounded source for the documented relation of teachers’ personal/professional selves to life, work, and context; it supports a careful cross-link to Teacher Identity Theory.

### A2. Definition, concepts, fit, and boundary

**L1 source facts.** Day's book is an authorised source for lifelong teacher development; Guskey directly frames a model of teacher change; Timperley's evidence synthesis distinguishes professional learning from the multiple conditions and outcomes it may involve.

**L2 page-ready definition.** “Teacher Professional Development Theory” should be renamed in explanatory prose as an **editorial entry point into plural professional-development and professional-learning models**, not a singular canonical theory. It asks: *How, under what workplace and learning conditions, do teachers develop knowledge, practice, commitments, or professional roles over time?* The page should name the chosen model before using it.

**L2 core concepts.** professional learning; opportunities and conditions for learning; knowledge/skills; changed practice; teacher beliefs/identity; learner outcomes; and professional career context. These are related analytic elements, not a guaranteed sequence. Guskey's model and Clarke–Hollingsworth's named domains are defensible, but specific to their models; Timperley supplies a context-sensitive evidence-synthesis route.

**L2 suitable questions.** Questions about teachers' learning opportunities, workplace conditions, changes in practice, professional role transitions, or how participants interpret professional learning. It is not the primary lens for a one-time student test with no teacher-learning mechanism, nor for claiming an intervention's impact without a matched design and evidence.

**L2 misuse/critique safeguards.** Do not call any workshop “development”; document what learning opportunity, practice, condition, and change claim are actually at issue. Do not infer student outcomes from self-reported satisfaction. Do not treat one model as an exhaustive account of professional learning, identity, organisational pressure, or career biography.

### A3. Evidence-supported relations, reading path, and verification

- **Relation to Teacher Identity Theory — L1/L2, source-bounded.** Day et al. (2006) links teachers’ personal/professional selves with life, work, and context. **L2 decision rule:** professional-development models organise learning/practice/change; identity theory examines how teachers understand and negotiate professional selves. Do not state that professional development automatically changes identity.
- **Relation to Communities of Practice — L2, source-bounded.** Communities of Practice can be a possible account of learning through participation; professional-development models are broader and may also concern formal opportunities, knowledge, practice, or evaluation. Do not label every training group a community of practice.
- **Relation to Life Course Theory — L2, source-bounded.** Life Course Theory may supply timing, pathways, linked lives, and historical context when the question is career development over time; it is not a substitute for a teacher-learning mechanism.

**Layered reading path.** (1) Day (1999) for scope and teacher development; (2) Clarke and Hollingsworth (2002) or Guskey (2002) for a named model; (3) Desimone (2009) and Timperley et al. (2007) for framework/evidence boundaries; (4) Day et al. (2006) when identity is actually part of the question.

## B. Teacher Life History Research

### B1. Source register and source-backed claims

1. Ivor F. Goodson, *Developing Narrative Theory: Life Histories and Personal Representation* (2013), [Routledge publisher record](https://www.routledge.com/Developing-Narrative-Theory-Life-Histories-and-Personal-Representation/Goodson/p/book/9780415603614). **L1:** authorised anchor for narrative theory, life histories, and personal representation.
2. Ivor F. Goodson (ed.), *Studying Teachers’ Lives* (1992), [Routledge publisher record](https://www.routledge.com/Studying-Teachers-Lives/Goodison/p/book/9780415068581). **L1:** an education-specific source collection addressing teachers’ backgrounds/professional histories, life-history theory/method, collaborative autobiography, and the approach’s problems/possibilities.
3. Ivor F. Goodson and Pat Sikes, *Life History Research in Educational Settings: Learning from Lives* (2001), [Open University Press / McGraw Hill record](https://www.mheducation.co.uk/life-history-research-in-educational-settings-9780335207138-emea). **L1:** education-specific book record for life-history research and the relation of lives to educational settings.
3. Patricia J. Josselson, “The Ethical Attitude in Narrative Research: Principles and Practicalities” (2007), in *Handbook of Narrative Inquiry*, [Springer DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-4614-5583-7_29). **L1:** source for treating narrative inquiry as ethically and relationally mediated rather than extracting transparent personal facts.

### B2. Definition, concepts, fit, and boundary

**L2 page-ready definition.** Teacher Life History Research is a **qualitative research tradition**, not a strict explanatory theory. It uses narrated biographies, memories, records, and social/historical context to interpret how teachers make professional meaning across lives and work. Its central question is: *How are teachers' professional accounts and turning points narrated, shaped, and situated in relationships, institutions, and historical contexts?*

**L1/L2 concepts.** Life history; personal representation; narration and memory; temporality; social and institutional context; researcher–participant relationship; and interpretation. Goodson is the source anchor for the first group; Josselson is the source anchor for relational/ethical limits. The page must distinguish an account from an independently verified event.

**L2 suitable questions.** Career stories, remembered turning points, professional trajectories, changing commitments, and the relationship between work biographies and wider educational/policy contexts. It is not a route to representative prevalence estimates, a replacement for documentary corroboration when a factual claim needs it, or an excuse to expose identifiable life details.

**L2 misuse/critique safeguards.** Do not treat memory as error-free or accounts as raw data waiting to be extracted. Attend to audience, time of telling, power, consent, anonymity, emotional risk, and the researcher's role in co-producing an account. Do not infer causal effects from a narrative sequence alone.

### B3. Evidence-supported relations, reading path, and verification

- **Relation to Life Course Theory — L2, source-bounded.** Both attend to time and biography, but their roles differ: life-course theory provides a theoretical vocabulary for pathways, timing, linked lives, and historical location; life-history research supplies an interpretive, narrative research tradition. Do not equate a life-history interview with evidence for every life-course principle.
- **Relation to Teacher Identity Theory — L2, source-bounded.** Narrative accounts can be suitable materials for studying professional self-understanding, but identity theory must specify the analytic mechanism; life-history research itself does not explain identity formation.
- **Relation to Structuration Theory — L2, source-bounded.** Structuration may help analyse how rules/resources are narrated as enabling or constraining practice. It should not be assumed merely because an interview mentions an institution.

**Layered reading path.** (1) Goodson (ed., 1992) for teacher-life research’s early problems/methods; (2) Goodson and Sikes (2001) for epistemology, educational context, ethics, and power; (3) Goodson (2013) for narrative representation; (4) Josselson (2007) for ethics and relational research practice.

| L2 | research-tradition definition, relations, fit and misuse boundaries | do not call the approach a causal theory |

## C. Educational Equity Theory

### C1. Source register and source-backed claims

1. UNESCO, *Global Education Monitoring Report 2020: Inclusion and Education—All Means All* (2020), [UNESCO record](https://unesdoc.unesco.org/ark:/48223/pf0000373718). **L1:** authoritative institutional source for the inclusion/equity context; it does not establish a singular “Educational Equity Theory.”
2. OECD, *Equity and Quality in Education: Supporting Disadvantaged Students and Schools* (2012), [OECD DOI](https://doi.org/10.1787/9789264130852-en). **L1:** authoritative policy evidence source concerning equity and quality; use only for its stated policy/education context, not as a universal theory.
3. Amartya Sen, *Inequality Reexamined* (1992), [Harvard University Press record](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674452560). **L1:** primary philosophical route to capability and equality framing; do not convert it into a directly tested education intervention claim.
4. Nancy Fraser, *Scales of Justice: Reimagining Political Space in a Globalizing World* (2008), [Columbia University Press record](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/scales-of-justice/9780231148726). **L1:** publisher-authorised route to redistribution, recognition, and representation as normative dimensions; this is a philosophical/political-theory resource, not one education-specific empirical model.

### C2. Definition, concepts, fit, and boundary

**L2 page-ready definition.** Educational Equity Theory is not a single classic theory. It is an **editorial umbrella** for empirically and normatively examining avoidable inequality in educational access, participation, treatment, resources, recognition, and outcomes. The page must state the selected normative basis (for example, capability, distribution, recognition, participation, rights, or an institutional policy standard) before evaluating a case as equitable or inequitable.

**L1/L2 concepts.** inclusion/exclusion; access; participation; resource distribution; recognition; outcome; and the comparison population. UNESCO supplies the institutional inclusion/equity context; Sen and Fraser offer distinct normative resources. Their coexistence is evidence against silently assuming one agreed metric.

**L2 suitable questions.** Which population is differently positioned, compared with whom, on which education dimension, through what rule/resource/interaction mechanism? This umbrella is not suitable as a neutral label for any group difference, a replacement for a causal design, or a claim that equal treatment is always equitable without a stated rationale.

**L2 misuse/critique safeguards.** Do not use “equity” as a slogan. Name the unit, population, comparator, dimension, normative basis, and hypothesised mechanism. Separate unequal outcomes from evidence about their causes. Avoid presenting a global institutional report as a canonical theory or treating disability, language, race, gender, class, geography, and migration as interchangeable categories.

### C3. Evidence-supported relations, reading path, and verification

- **Relation to Social Capital Theory — L2, source-bounded.** Social-capital analysis can specify a relation-enabled resource-access mechanism within an equity question. It does not itself decide what an equitable distribution should be; equity research must supply the normative/comparative standard.
- **Relation to Bourdieu's Practice Theory — L2, source-bounded.** Bourdieu can provide a relational account of capital, recognition, and field-specific advantage; an equity page must still state its normative claim rather than treating any unequal capital as self-evidently the entire equity analysis.
- **Relation to Institutional Theory — L2, source-bounded.** Institutional theory may help investigate how rules, legitimacy pressures, and organisational arrangements shape access or participation, but it does not by itself define equity.

**Layered reading path.** (1) UNESCO (2020) for institutional orientation; (2) OECD (2012) for policy/education context; (3) Sen (1992) and Fraser (2008) to make the chosen normative basis explicit and distinguish distribution, capability, recognition, or representation.

| Level | Publishable item | Review condition |
| --- | --- | --- |
| L1 | source records and their limited institutional/philosophical claims | cite UNESCO/OECD/Sen/Fraser separately; do not attribute an umbrella definition to one source |
| L2 | editorial-umbrella definition, comparisons, fit and boundary statements | expose the normative basis and comparison rather than presenting a consensus metric |
| L3 | data and chapter suggestions | mark as context-specific and avoid causal guarantees |

## D. Institutional Theory

### D1. Source register and source-backed claims

1. John W. Meyer and Brian Rowan, “Institutionalized Organizations: Formal Structure as Myth and Ceremony” (1977), *American Journal of Sociology*, 83(2), 340–363, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/226550). **L1:** formal structures may reflect rationalised institutional rules; organisations can seek legitimacy/resources/stability and decouple formal structures from ongoing activity.
2. Paul J. DiMaggio and Walter W. Powell, “The Iron Cage Revisited: Institutional Isomorphism and Collective Rationality in Organizational Fields” (1983), *American Sociological Review*, 48(2), 147–160, [JSTOR stable record](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2095101). **L1:** central organisational neo-institutionalist source for organisational fields and institutional isomorphism.
3. Stephen R. Barley and Pamela S. Tolbert, “Institutionalization and Structuration: Studying the Links between Action and Institution” (1997), *Organization Studies*, 18(1), 93–117, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/017084069701800106). **L1:** explicitly compares institutional and structuration theory, treating institutions/action as linked and proposing institutionalisation as a dynamic process.
4. W. Richard Scott, *Institutions and Organizations: Ideas, Interests, and Identities* (4th ed., 2014), [SAGE publisher record](https://us.sagepub.com/en-us/nam/institutions-and-organizations/book242059). **L1:** a publisher-authorised orientation route to the plural institutional tradition; do not cite it as an original source for every earlier claim.

### D2. Definition, concepts, fit, and boundary

**L2 page-ready definition.** Institutional Theory is a **family of approaches**, and this D1 page should identify its anchor as organisational neo-institutionalism. It examines how institutionalised rules, cultural expectations, fields, and legitimacy pressures shape organisational structures and practices, including possible decoupling between formal arrangements and ongoing work. Its core question is: *Which institutional rules, expectations, and field relations make an organisational form or practice appear legitimate, and how is it adopted, maintained, enacted, or altered?*

**L1/L2 concepts.** institutionally rationalised rules; legitimacy; organisational field; isomorphism; formal structure; decoupling; and institutionalisation. Meyer/Rowan directly support rules, myths, legitimacy, and decoupling; DiMaggio/Powell is the source anchor for isomorphism/fields. C4 must distinguish coercive, mimetic, and normative mechanisms only where the cited classic source is attached and empirical evidence matches the asserted mechanism.

**L2 suitable questions.** Organisational conformity or variation, legitimacy, adoption of formal structures, field expectations, decoupling, and institutionalisation/change. It is not a primary explanation for a purely individual decision with no organisational/field setting, nor is a resemblance between organisations sufficient evidence for a particular institutional pressure.

**L2 misuse/critique safeguards.** Do not call all context “institutional.” Do not infer coercion, imitation, professional norm, or decoupling from similarity alone. Do not describe organisations as passive “cultural dopes”; document actors, timing, rules, field relations, and the difference between formal arrangement and enacted practice. Do not treat a named organisation as the whole organisational field.

### D3. Evidence-supported relations, reading path, and verification

- **Relation to Structuration Theory — L1/L2.** Barley and Tolbert directly state that both traditions link institutions and action and argue that a fusion can advance empirical study of institutionalisation. **L2 decision rule:** use institutional theory when legitimacy, field expectations, and organisational form are primary; add structuration when recursive enactment of rules/resources and change through practice needs its own mechanism. Do not claim the two are identical.
- **Relation to Bourdieu's Practice Theory — L2, source-bounded.** Both can analyse organisations beyond efficiency; Bourdieu's approach instead centres field positions, capital, habitus, and symbolic power. Use the latter where unequal positions/recognition are central, rather than relabelling all institutional pressure as field logic.
- **Relation to Communities of Practice — L2, source-bounded.** CoP can examine situated learning and participation inside/around an organisation; institutional theory can analyse legitimacy and field conditions. Neither implies the other.

**Layered reading path.** (1) Meyer and Rowan (1977) for formal structure, legitimacy, and decoupling; (2) DiMaggio and Powell (1983) for fields/isomorphism; (3) Barley and Tolbert (1997) for action–institution dynamics and comparison with structuration; Scott (2014) as a plural-tradition orientation.

| --- | --- | --- |
| L1 | source metadata and limited rules/legitimacy/decoupling/isomorphism claims | attach the exact classic source; distinguish source claims from later synthesis |
| L2 | family definition, theory choice, relations, fit and misuse boundaries | name organisational neo-institutionalism and do not universalise one strand |
| L3 | document/interview/field-map suggestions | label as options; require evidence for the claimed mechanism |

## E. Street-Level Bureaucracy

### E1. Source register and source-backed claims

1. Michael Lipsky, *Street-Level Bureaucracy: Dilemmas of the Individual in Public Services* (30th anniversary expanded ed., 2010; original 1980), [Russell Sage Foundation record](https://www.russellsage.org/publications/book/street-level-bureaucracy). **L1:** frontline public-service workers exercise discretion in day-to-day implementation; the source describes high caseloads, ambiguous agency goals, inadequate resources, routines/simplifications, and policy adaptations.
2. Michael Lipsky, “Toward a Theory of Street-Level Bureaucracy” (1998 reprint record), [U.S. Office of Justice Programs record](https://www.ojp.gov/ncjrs/virtual-library/abstracts/toward-theory-street-level-bureaucracy-criminal-justice-system). **L1:** identifies direct interaction with clients, substantial discretion, inadequate resources, contested authority, and contradictory/ambiguous expectations as conditions central to the formulation.
3. Deborah Rice, “Street-Level Bureaucrats and the Welfare State: Toward a Micro-Institutionalist Theory of Policy Implementation” (2013), *Administration & Society*, 45(9), 1038–1062, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/0095399712451895). **L1:** directly argues for linking the Lipsky approach with institutionalist theory in complex implementation settings; do not convert this proposal into a universal merger.
4. Julia Dahlvik, “Asylum as Construction Work: Theorizing Administrative Practices” (2017), *Migration Studies*, 5(3), 369–388, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1093/migration/mnx043). **L1:** a peer-reviewed application combining street-level bureaucracy with structuration theory; use it only to demonstrate a documented possible combination.

### E2. Definition, concepts, fit, and boundary

**L2 page-ready definition.** Street-Level Bureaucracy is a **public-policy/administration theoretical perspective** on how frontline public workers implement policy through case-level interaction and discretion under organisational constraints. It asks: *How do resource pressures, ambiguous goals, routines, relationships, and discretionary judgement shape policy as it is actually delivered?*

**L1/L2 concepts.** street-level bureaucrats; direct client interaction; discretion; resource scarcity/caseloads; ambiguous or conflicting goals; coping/routinisation; and implementation as policy in practice. Lipsky directly supports the core conditions and policy-adaptation focus. The page should describe coping practices as responses to conditions to be investigated, not as moral failings or universal behaviour.

**L2 suitable questions.** Frontline implementation of education, welfare, social, policing, licensing, or other public services where employees interact with clients and make consequential case-level judgements under identifiable constraints. It is not a theory of agenda setting before implementation, of back-office systems with no comparable discretionary client interaction, or of individual misconduct without an implementation context.

**L2 misuse/critique safeguards.** Do not label every teacher, platform worker, or service employee a street-level bureaucrat merely because they serve people. Establish public-policy implementation, client interaction, discretion, and the organisational conditions at issue. Do not assume discretion always equals autonomy, bias, resistance, or innovation; trace the rule, condition, judgement, routine, and result. Do not export the original institutional setting to other states or services without examining governance and resource context.

### E3. Evidence-supported relations, reading path, and verification

- **Relation to Institutional Theory — L1/L2.** Rice directly proposes a micro-institutionalist account connecting street-level bureaucracy and institutionalist theory. **L2 decision rule:** use street-level bureaucracy for the case-level implementation mechanism, and institutional theory only when field/organisational rules, logics, legitimacy, or institutional conditions are separately evidenced.
- **Relation to Structuration Theory — L1/L2.** Dahlvik documents an application combining structuration and street-level perspectives. **L2 decision rule:** the latter explains frontline implementation dilemmas; structuration can add a rule/resource and recursive-enactment account. The application is not proof that every frontline study needs both.
- **Relation to Multiple Streams Framework — L2, source-bounded.** The scope differs in time and analysis: MSF's original problem is agenda setting, while Lipsky's is implementation. A sequential study may use MSF for agenda/decision development and street-level bureaucracy for later delivery, but must not claim either framework explains the other's stage without additional theory and evidence.

**Layered reading path.** (1) Lipsky (2010) for the classic formulation; (2) Lipsky's OJP record for defining conditions; (3) Rice (2013) for a bounded institutional-theory connection; (4) Dahlvik (2017) for one documented structuration combination.

| L2 | definition, fit/non-fit, stage distinction, and safeguards | do not make discretion universally positive/negative or conflate frameworks |
| L3 | case tracing, interview, document, observation, or chapter suggestions | frame as context-dependent and protect staff/client confidentiality |

## F. Multiple Streams Framework

### F1. Source register and source-backed claims

1. John W. Kingdon, *Agendas, Alternatives, and Public Policies*, updated 2nd ed. (2011; original 1984), [Pearson publisher record](https://www.pearson.com/en-gb/subject-catalog/p/agendas-alternatives-and-public-policies-update-edition-with-an-epilogue-on-health-care-pearson-new-international-edition/P200000004628?view=educator). **L1:** publisher describes agenda setting, actors, alternatives, and the question of how issues reach officials' attention; its underlying empirical focus is U.S. federal-government agenda setting.
2. Nikolaos Zahariadis, “Multiple Streams Framework” (2023), *Encyclopedia of Public Policy*, [Springer DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-030-90434-0_70-1). **L1:** identifies MSF as a policy-process perspective introduced by Kingdon to explain why issues rise on the U.S. federal agenda; it foregrounds attention, search, selection, timing, and ambiguity.
3. Nicole Herweg, Nikolaos Zahariadis, and Reimut Zohlnhöfer, “The Multiple Streams Framework,” in *Foundations, Refinements, and Empirical Applications of the Multiple Streams Framework* (2018), [Taylor & Francis DOI](https://doi.org/10.4324/9780429494284-2). **L1:** publisher-authorised source for the framework's structural elements, later refinements, applications, and limitations.
4. Nicole Herweg, Nikolaos Zahariadis, and Reimut Zohlnhöfer, “Travelling Far and Wide? Applying the Multiple Streams Framework to Policy-Making in Autocracies” (2022), *Politische Vierteljahresschrift*, 63(2), 203–223, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11615-022-00393-8). **L1:** a direct source for the boundary that transfer across political systems needs explicit theoretical adaptation rather than automatic portability.
5. Reimut Zohlnhöfer, Nicole Herweg, and Nikolaos Zahariadis, “How to Conduct a Multiple Streams Study” (2022), in *Methods of the Policy Process*, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.4324/9781003269083-2). **L1:** a traceable methods-reading route; do not treat it as a universal procedure.

### F2. Definition, concepts, fit, and boundary

**L2 page-ready definition.** The Multiple Streams Framework is an **analytical policy-process framework/perspective**, not a universal causal law. It explains agenda change and policy choice under ambiguity by examining the partly independent development and temporary coupling of problem, policy, and political streams, often with policy entrepreneurs and policy windows. Its core question is: *How and when do conditions, proposals, political circumstances, and entrepreneurial action become coupled so that an issue receives agenda attention or becomes actionable?*

**L1/L2 concepts.** problem stream; policy stream; politics stream; policy entrepreneurs; policy windows; coupling; ambiguity; and temporal sequence. Kingdon and Zahariadis are the L1 anchors for agenda setting/attention and the core framework. C4 must not reduce a stream to a retrospective label inferred from the policy outcome.

**L2 suitable questions.** Agenda setting, issue attention, policy formulation/choice, temporary windows, and the timing of coupling where dated evidence of the streams and actors can be assembled. It is not, by itself, the primary framework for frontline delivery, a general theory of policy effectiveness, or an after-the-fact story explaining any adopted policy.

**L2 misuse/critique safeguards.** Do not name three streams once the outcome is already known. Trace their distinct development, relevant participants, timing, and an evidenced coupling event. State jurisdiction, institutional setting, and policy stage. Kingdon's original U.S. agenda-setting focus does not automatically transfer to other political systems, levels, or implementation stages; use the later sources to justify and bound any extension.

### F3. Evidence-supported relations, reading path, and verification

- **Relation to Street-Level Bureaucracy — L2, source-bounded.** MSF is primarily an agenda-setting/policy-choice lens; street-level bureaucracy is an implementation lens. A process-tracing study can divide explanatory labour across stages, but must include evidence between agenda decision and frontline delivery rather than assuming the stages connect automatically.
- **Relation to Institutional Theory — L1/L2.** Herweg et al. (2022) directly examine how MSF travels across political systems and foreground the need to address institutional context. **L2 decision rule:** institutional theory can analyse legitimacy, organisational fields, and rule environments; MSF can analyse agenda coupling. They are complementary only when the question needs both, not interchangeable.
- **Relation to Life Course Theory — L2, source-bounded.** Both attend to timing, but their units differ: MSF addresses policy processes/windows; life-course theory addresses biographies/pathways. Shared vocabulary is not a theoretical integration.

**Layered reading path.** (1) Kingdon (2011 [1984]) for origin and U.S. agenda-setting problem; (2) Zahariadis (2023) for a concise publisher-authorised framework orientation; (3) Herweg et al. (2018) and Zohlnhöfer et al. (2022) for refinements/methods; (4) Herweg et al. (2022) before applying the framework across political systems.
