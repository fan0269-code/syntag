## A. Practice Theory (Bourdieu)

### A1. Source register

1. Pierre Bourdieu, *Outline of a Theory of Practice* (1977, translated by Richard Nice), [Cambridge University Press record and DOI](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511812507). The publisher describes the book as a theory of practice that addresses the relation between objective structures, habitus, practice, reproduction, symbolic capital, and symbolic power.
2. Pierre Bourdieu, *The Logic of Practice* (1990, translated by Richard Nice), [Stanford University Press record](https://www.sup.org/books/sociology/logic-practice). The press describes it as Bourdieu's fullest statement of the theory and explicitly identifies habitus, structure/practice, symbolic capital, domination, reflexivity, and Algeria-based ethnographic cases.
3. Pierre Bourdieu, “The Forms of Capital” (1986), pp. 241–258 in *Handbook of Theory and Research for the Sociology of Education*, edited by J. G. Richardson, Greenwood Press; [University of Illinois library-hosted bibliographic PDF](https://publish.illinois.edu/crittheory/files/2023/01/Bourdieu-The-Forms-of-Capital.pdf). This is the classic English-language source for economic, cultural, and social capital and their convertibility.
4. Pierre Bourdieu and Loïc J. D. Wacquant, *An Invitation to Reflexive Sociology* (1992), [University of Chicago Press record](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/I/bo3649674.html). Its table of contents and description provide an authoritative access point for relationalism, field, habitus, rationality, symbolic violence, reflexivity, and objections to Bourdieu's work.
5. Pierre Bourdieu, “Symbolic Power” (1979), *Critique of Anthropology*, 4(13–14), [SAGE journal record and DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/0308275X7900401307). This source supports treating symbolic power and recognition as analytic concerns rather than assuming that only material resources matter.
6. Pierre Bourdieu, *Forms of Capital: General Sociology, Volume 3* (2021 English edition of 1983–84 lectures), [Wiley publisher record](https://www.wiley-vch.de/en/areas-interest/humanities-social-sciences/forms-of-capital-978-1-5095-2670-3). The publisher describes capital, habitus, and field as the trilogy of concepts central to the approach and characterises field as relatively durable relations among agents and institutions with specific stakes and investments.

### A2. Accurate definition and core problem

**L1 source facts.** Cambridge describes *Outline* as developing habitus to mediate between objective structures and practices, and as addressing reproduction, symbolic capital, and domination. Stanford describes *The Logic of Practice* as a fuller statement that works through the relation between structure and practice, practical mastery, habitus, symbolic capital, and modes of domination ([Cambridge](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511812507); [Stanford](https://www.sup.org/books/sociology/logic-practice)).

**L2 page-ready definition.** Bourdieu's practice theory is a relational social theory for explaining how patterned practice is generated and contested through the relation among **habitus** (socially formed dispositions), **capital** (resources with differential value), and **field** (a structured arena of positions, stakes, and struggles). It asks: *How do historically formed dispositions and unequal resources meet the rules, positions, and stakes of a field to make some practices more likely, intelligible, valued, or powerful than others?*

This definition must not imply that practice is mechanically determined by origin, that fields have fixed borders, or that every social interaction is a field-level struggle.

### A3. Intellectual lineage, scholars, and representative works

**L1.** Bourdieu is the principal theorist of the named approach. The Cambridge and Stanford records identify *Outline* and *Logic* as central theoretical statements; the University of Chicago Press record identifies Wacquant as a collaborator and interpreter in a systematic presentation of Bourdieu's thought, not as the originator of Bourdieu's theory ([Cambridge](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511812507); [Stanford](https://www.sup.org/books/sociology/logic-practice); [Chicago](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/I/bo3649674.html)).

| Scholar / work | Directly supported contribution | Wording boundary |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pierre Bourdieu — *Outline of a Theory of Practice* (1977) | Brings together structures, habitus, practical logic, symbolic power, and reproduction. | Do not make this one book the source of every later “practice theory” tradition. |
| Pierre Bourdieu — *The Logic of Practice* (1990) | Elaborates the critique of objectivism/subjectivism and uses ethnographic cases to develop practical logic. | Algerian cases are foundational examples, not evidence that all fields operate identically. |
| Pierre Bourdieu — “The Forms of Capital” (1986) | Sets out economic, cultural, and social capital and makes conversion/recognition central to the account. | Do not turn every credential, contact, or taste into capital without showing field-specific value and convertibility. |
| Pierre Bourdieu and Loïc Wacquant — *An Invitation to Reflexive Sociology* (1992) | Gives an accessible, source-authorised route to field, habitus, relationalism, symbolic violence, and reflexivity. | Wacquant is a coauthor/interpreter here, not a co-founder of the original theory. |

**L2 genealogy.** The approach works against a simple opposition between objectivist structure and voluntarist subject. It draws on structural analysis and phenomenological attention to practical action, but Bourdieu's distinct analytic vocabulary is habitus–capital–field. It should be presented as Bourdieu's specific theory of practice, not as a synonym for all “practice-oriented” scholarship.

### A4. Six core concepts and their relation

1. **Habitus — L1/L2.** Cambridge and Stanford identify habitus as the concept mediating structures and practices ([Cambridge](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511812507); [Stanford](https://www.sup.org/books/sociology/logic-practice)). **L2 operational wording:** durable but revisable dispositions—perception, judgment, bodily and practical tendencies—formed through prior conditions and encounters.
2. **Field — L1/L2.** The Wiley record defines a field as relatively durable relations among agents and institutions, a site of specific investments, forms of capital, and material/symbolic profits ([Wiley](https://www.wiley-vch.de/en/areas-interest/humanities-social-sciences/forms-of-capital-978-1-5095-2670-3)). **L2:** identify positions, stakes, rules of recognition, and struggles rather than treating “school,” “university,” or “organisation” as a field merely because it is an institution.
3. **Capital — L1.** The 1986 source distinguishes economic, cultural, and social capital; the Wiley record presents capital as one of the approach's central concepts ([Illinois/Greenwood record](https://publish.illinois.edu/crittheory/files/2023/01/Bourdieu-The-Forms-of-Capital.pdf); [Wiley](https://www.wiley-vch.de/en/areas-interest/humanities-social-sciences/forms-of-capital-978-1-5095-2670-3)).
4. **Symbolic capital / recognition — L1/L2.** Cambridge and Stanford connect the theory to symbolic capital and symbolic power; the SAGE record is a direct route to symbolic power ([Cambridge](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511812507); [Stanford](https://www.sup.org/books/sociology/logic-practice); [SAGE](https://doi.org/10.1177/0308275X7900401307)). **L2:** resources have effects partly because they are recognised as legitimate in a given field.
5. **Practical sense / practice — L1/L2.** Stanford distinguishes practical from merely logical mastery and centres the logic of everyday practice ([Stanford](https://www.sup.org/books/sociology/logic-practice)). **L2:** analysis should consider situated improvisation and judgment, not presume actors consciously calculate every outcome.
6. **Doxa, struggle, and symbolic violence — L1/L2.** The Chicago record documents field logic, symbolic violence, and reflexivity as central topics ([Chicago](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/I/bo3649674.html)). **L2:** study which classifications and standards become taken for granted, who benefits, and where they are contested; do not presume domination before examining the case.

**L2 relationship model.** Prior social conditions help form habitus; a field organises positions, stakes, and what counts as valuable; uneven capital shapes the positions and strategies available; recognition can convert resources into symbolic advantage; practical sense guides action under these conditions; doxa and struggle concern whether the field's classifications are reproduced or contested. This is an analytic model, **not** a universal linear causal sequence.

### A5. Main candidate and alternative theory comparisons

| Comparison | Shared concern | Important difference | Decision aid |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Structuration Theory** (main candidate) | Both refuse a simple structure-versus-agency split and attend to recurrent social practice. | Structuration foregrounds the duality of structure, rules, resources, and recursive enactment. Bourdieu foregrounds habitus, unequal capital, field positions, recognition, and symbolic domination. | Use Bourdieu when unequal resources and position-taking are central; use Structuration when the study's main mechanism is how actors recursively draw on and reproduce rules/resources. |
| **Communities of Practice** (alternative) | Both analyse practice as social rather than only individual skill. | Communities of Practice foregrounds participation, learning, membership, competence, and shared repertoire. Bourdieu can analyse these but more directly asks how field position, capital, classification, and recognition organise unequal participation. | Use CoP when learning through participation is the primary object; use Bourdieu when a “community” must be situated in wider stratification or competition. |
| **Social Capital Theory** (adjacent alternative) | Both make relationships and resources analytically important. | Bourdieu treats social capital as one form in a wider relational account of capital, field, conversion, and domination. Social-capital theories may instead focus on relational resources, closure, access, or network position without a full field analysis. | Do not call network ties “Bourdieu” if habitus, field, and capital conversion are absent. |

All distinctions in this table are **L2 editorial comparisons** informed by the source-defined Bourdieu vocabulary. They are not claims that the theories cannot be combined.

### A6. Suitable questions, non-fit, and boundaries

**L2 suitable questions.**

- How do students, teachers, or professionals from differently resourced backgrounds acquire and have particular credentials, styles, networks, or expertise recognised in an educational field?
- How do admissions, promotion, evaluation, or professional standards distribute symbolic recognition and reproduce or challenge field positions?
- How do people develop practical judgment while moving between social worlds or institutions with different stakes and classificatory schemes?
- How do economic, cultural, social, and symbolic resources become convertible—or fail to become convertible—in a particular field?
- How are apparently individual choices connected to trajectories, dispositions, opportunities, and constraints without treating them as predetermined?

**L2 usually non-fit as the primary theory.**

- A study asking only whether a training intervention changes a narrowly defined performance score, with no question about practice, position, resources, or recognition.
- A technical workflow analysis whose relevant mechanism is physical process reliability rather than social relations, classification, or unequal resources.
- A study of short-lived interaction with no defensible field, history, capital, or disposition question.
- A project that labels a demographic category as “habitus” but collects no evidence about dispositions, trajectories, practices, or position.
- A purely individual psychological mechanism for which relationships, institutional positions, and social differentiation are outside scope.

### A7. Common misuse, critique, and safeguards

1. **Habitus as destiny — L2.** Treat habitus as historically formed and practically generative, not as a fixed trait that erases improvisation, conflict, learning, or change.
2. **Capital by label — L1/L2.** The forms of capital are analytic resources, not demographic proxies. Document the resource, its acquisition, its field-specific value, and any conversion or recognition before naming it capital ([Illinois/Greenwood record](https://publish.illinois.edu/crittheory/files/2023/01/Bourdieu-The-Forms-of-Capital.pdf)).
3. **Institution equals field — L2.** Establish positions, stakes, relations, investments, and criteria of value; a named organisation is not automatically a bounded field.
4. **Symbolic violence assumed in advance — L1/L2.** Symbolic power is a genuine part of the theoretical vocabulary ([SAGE](https://doi.org/10.1177/0308275X7900401307)), but a study must show classifications, recognition, and their effects rather than treating every hierarchy as already explained.
5. **Concept inventory without mechanism — L2.** Listing habitus, field, and capital is not a framework. State how the proposed field conditions make some capital valuable and how habitus/practical sense enters observable practice.
6. **Researcher exempt from the field — L1/L3.** Reflexivity is integral to the approach ([Chicago](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/I/bo3649674.html)). Researchers should consider their own categories, access, and position; this is not a guarantee of neutrality.
7. **Universalising one field — L2.** Do not transport a classification scheme from elite higher education, art, or one national system into another case without checking its local history and stakes.

### A8. From concepts to research design

The table is **L3 guidance**. Select dimensions only when they answer the stated question, and distinguish participant claims, documentary evidence, and researcher inference.

| Concept | Possible analysis dimension | Possible materials | Claim boundary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Habitus | dispositions, practical classifications, trajectory-linked judgment, bodily or linguistic cues where ethically appropriate | episode interviews, life histories, recurring decisions, observed routines | Do not infer habitus from one quote or social category alone. |
| Field | positions, entrants/incumbents, stakes, rules of recognition, institutional history | policy documents, criteria, organisation charts, interviews across positions, archives | Do not treat formal hierarchy as the whole field. |
| Capital | resource type, acquisition, convertibility, scarcity, field value | credentials, portfolios, referrals, records, networks, participant accounts | Having a resource does not prove it is valued or converted. |
| Symbolic recognition | legitimacy, status attribution, classification, misrecognition | evaluation texts, award/promotion records, meetings, interactional accounts | Do not infer recognition only from self-reported prestige. |
| Practice / practical sense | routines, improvisations, taken-for-granted distinctions | observation, stimulated recall, workflow artefacts, cases of disruption | Observation does not by itself establish prior trajectory or disposition. |
| Doxa / struggle | what appears natural, contested criteria, boundary work | controversies, policy changes, meeting records, comparisons across positions | Do not call all disagreement a field struggle. |

**L3 data suggestions.** Interviews can trace trajectories, resource acquisition, classification, and moments of practical judgment; pair them with documents or observations when claims concern field rules or recognition. Observation can reveal enactment and interactional classification, but not automatically an actor's disposition or history. Documents can show rules, credentials, and official classifications, but should not be equated with lived practice. Network materials may be relevant for social capital, but should be joined to field position and the actual value of ties. Comparative cases can test whether the same resource has different value across fields, but they require defensible case selection.

**L3 chapter use.** In a literature review, distinguish Bourdieu's specific theory from wider practice-oriented work. In the framework, specify the relevant field, the capital at issue, the proposed relation to habitus/practice, and rival explanations. In methods, explain how position, history, and recognition will be evidenced and how researcher reflexivity is handled. In findings, show counter-cases and failed conversions of capital. In discussion, separate evidence of patterned association from evidence of a proposed reproductive mechanism.

## B. Social Capital Theory

### B1. Source register

1. James S. Coleman, “Social Capital in the Creation of Human Capital” (1988), *American Journal of Sociology*, 94 supplement, S95–S120, DOI [10.1086/228943](https://doi.org/10.1086/228943); [Oxford Academic republication record](https://doi.org/10.1093/oso/9780195159509.003.0007). The source introduces social capital as a conceptual tool for bringing social structure into a rational-action account; the original article discusses forms, conditions of creation, and an education application.
2. Pierre Bourdieu, “The Forms of Capital” (1986), [University of Illinois library-hosted bibliographic PDF](https://publish.illinois.edu/crittheory/files/2023/01/Bourdieu-The-Forms-of-Capital.pdf). This is an independent classic formulation of social capital in relation to durable networks, group membership, other capital forms, and conversion.
3. Alejandro Portes, “Social Capital: Its Origins and Applications in Modern Sociology” (1998), *Annual Review of Sociology*, 24, 1–24, DOI [10.1146/annurev.soc.24.1.1](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev.soc.24.1.1). Its abstract reviews Bourdieu, Loury, Coleman and others; distinguishes sources, functions, negative consequences, and conceptual stretching.
4. Nan Lin, *Social Capital: A Theory of Social Structure and Action* (2001), [Cambridge University Press record and DOI](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815447). Cambridge describes the approach as resources accessed through social connections and relations and situates it in the interaction of individual action and social structure.
5. Michael Woolcock, “Social Capital and Economic Development: Toward a Theoretical Synthesis and Policy Framework” (1998), *Theory and Society*, 27, 151–208, DOI [10.1023/A:1006884930135](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A%3A1006884930135). The Springer record verifies the publication, author, title, and policy/theoretical-synthesis focus.
6. Pierre Bourdieu, *Forms of Capital: General Sociology, Volume 3* (2021 English edition), [Wiley publisher record](https://www.wiley-vch.de/en/areas-interest/humanities-social-sciences/forms-of-capital-978-1-5095-2670-3). It gives a publisher-authorised account of capital, field, investments, and material/symbolic profits.

### B2. Accurate definition and core problem

**L1 source facts.** Coleman presents social capital as a conceptual tool that links social structure to action ([Oxford Academic](https://doi.org/10.1093/oso/9780195159509.003.0007)). Cambridge describes Lin's formulation as resources accessed through social connections and relations, relevant to individuals, groups, organisations, and communities in pursuing objectives ([Cambridge](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815447)). Portes explicitly reviews competing definitions, positive functions, negative consequences, and conceptual extension from individual asset to community or national feature ([Annual Reviews](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev.soc.24.1.1)).

**L2 page-ready definition.** Social Capital Theory is best presented as a **family of related but non-identical theories**, not a single settled measure. It examines how relationships, network structure, membership, norms, obligations, recognition, and trust may make resources, information, support, coordination, or opportunities accessible to actors. Its core question is: *Which social relations make which resources available to whom, through what mechanism, under what conditions, and with what unequal or negative consequences?*

The page must state which tradition it uses—e.g., Bourdieu, Coleman, Lin, or a collective/civic formulation—before treating a variable as “social capital.”

### B3. Lineage, scholars, and representative works

| Scholar / work | Directly supported contribution | Wording boundary |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pierre Bourdieu — “The Forms of Capital” (1986) | Places social capital within a wider account of economic, cultural, social, and symbolic capital, accumulation, conversion, and group membership. | Do not detach Bourdieu's social capital from field, conversion, recognition, and unequal positions. |
| James S. Coleman — “Social Capital in the Creation of Human Capital” (1988) | Introduces social capital as a tool for placing social structure in an action account; identifies forms and conditions and illustrates it in education. | Do not equate Coleman's formulation with every network metric or all later civic-capital claims. |
| Nan Lin — *Social Capital* (2001) | Builds a social-structure/action theory around resources accessed through social connections and relations. | Do not treat access, mobilisation, and outcome as interchangeable measures. |
| Alejandro Portes — “Social Capital…” (1998) | Reviews origins/definitions, sources, positive functions, negative consequences, and conceptual stretching. | A review and critique is not one more universal definition. |
| Michael Woolcock — “Social Capital and Economic Development…” (1998) | Provides a theoretical-synthesis/policy-framework contribution, especially relevant when the unit is development or institutional context. | Do not import development-level claims into individual or classroom studies without a matching unit and mechanism. |

**L2 genealogy.** The concept has at least several enduring traditions: Bourdieu's relational account of capital and stratification; Coleman's social-structural resource-for-action account; Lin's network-resource and access/mobilisation account; and collective, civic, organisational, or development-oriented uses. These traditions overlap but are not interchangeable. Portes's review is the central warning against conceptual stretch ([Annual Reviews](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev.soc.24.1.1)).

### B4. Six core concepts and relations

1. **Ties, membership, and relational location — L1/L2.** Bourdieu's and Lin's source records place capital in relations/connections and group membership ([Illinois/Greenwood record](https://publish.illinois.edu/crittheory/files/2023/01/Bourdieu-The-Forms-of-Capital.pdf); [Cambridge](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815447)). **L2:** define the relevant relation and its setting rather than counting all contacts together.
2. **Resources accessible through relations — L1.** Lin's publisher description directly specifies resources accessed through connections and relations ([Cambridge](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815447)).
3. **Access, mobilisation, and return — L2.** A defensible design distinguishes resources that are potentially reachable, resources actually activated, and the outcome that follows; these are not one variable. This makes Lin's resource-access framing usable without treating access as automatic benefit.
4. **Obligations, norms, trust, and closure — L1/L2.** Coleman's article is concerned with social structure, norms, rules, and obligations within a theory of action ([Oxford Academic](https://doi.org/10.1093/oso/9780195159509.003.0007)). **L2:** these are possible mechanisms, not universal ingredients; specify whether the proposed mechanism is obligation, information, sanction, brokerage, recognition, or something else.
5. **Network structure and position — L1/L2.** Lin theorises social capital at the intersection of action and social structure ([Cambridge](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815447)). **L2:** density, closure, bridging, centrality, and brokerage can be useful structural descriptions only when their relationship to resource flow or coordination is theorised.
6. **Inequality, exclusion, and negative consequences — L1.** Portes says the same social processes can produce negative consequences and warns that conceptual extension can jeopardise heuristic value ([Annual Reviews](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev.soc.24.1.1)).

**L2 relationship model.** Relationship and network position can condition access to particular resources; norms, obligation, trust, recognition, or brokerage may mediate whether resources are mobilised; conversion into an outcome depends on institutional rules, other capital, and the actor's position. The same closure or network may support insiders and exclude outsiders. This is not a claim that every tie produces benefit.

### B5. Main candidate and alternative theory comparisons

| Comparison | Shared concern | Important difference | Decision aid |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Bourdieu's Practice Theory** (main candidate) | Both treat relationships and resources as socially consequential. | In Bourdieu, social capital is one capital form whose value/conversion depends on field, habitus, symbolic recognition, and stratification. A social-capital study may focus more narrowly on ties, access, obligations, or network position. | Use Bourdieu when social capital must be explained within field-level power, capitals, and dispositions; use a specified social-capital tradition when the central mechanism is relation-enabled resource access. |
| **Network / structural-relational analysis** (alternative) | Both require attention to network relations and position. | Network analysis is a set of descriptive and inferential tools; it does not itself establish what counts as capital, what resource is accessed, or whether an outcome is beneficial. | Use network measures to operationalise a clear social-capital mechanism; do not use a graph alone as evidence of “high social capital.” |
| **Communities of Practice** (additional alternative) | Both can explain why relationships matter for access and learning. | CoP focuses on participation, competence, identity, and shared practice. Social capital focuses on relation-enabled resources, information, obligations, and opportunity; a group can display either without the other. | Use CoP for learning through participation; use social capital for resource access/coordination; combine only with a stated division of explanatory labour. |

These are **L2 editorial comparisons**. They do not prohibit mixed frameworks; they require the study to state which theory answers which part of the question.

### B6. Suitable questions, non-fit, and boundaries

**L2 suitable questions.**

- Which relationships give students, teachers, researchers, or organisations access to information, mentoring, referrals, support, or opportunities—and which do not?
- How do networks, obligations, norms, and institutional rules shape mobilisation of resources during transition, collaboration, or professional development?
- How do relational advantages and exclusions distribute unevenly across positions, communities, or organisational boundaries?
- Does a bridging, closure, brokerage, or membership mechanism plausibly explain variation in access or coordination, after accounting for other resources and institutional conditions?
- How do group membership and recognition convert relationships into support, influence, or opportunities in a named setting?

**L2 usually non-fit as the primary theory.**

- A study of an individual's skill or attitude when neither relational access nor a social-structural mechanism is part of the question.
- A claim about general trust, happiness, or civic virtue with no identified relation, resource, mechanism, or unit of analysis.
- A study where a contact count is the only evidence but resource quality, access, use, inequality, and outcome are never examined.
- A causal evaluation that requires a design for treatment effects but uses “social capital” merely as a positive background explanation.
- A question about how everyday dispositions are formed and valued in a power-laden field when a Bourdieuian field/habitus account is the actual theoretical need.

### B7. Misuse, critique, and safeguards

1. **One label, many theories — L1/L2.** Portes documents different definitions and warns against conceptual stretch ([Annual Reviews](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev.soc.24.1.1)). Name the tradition and unit before choosing indicators.
2. **Tie count as benefit — L2.** More contacts do not establish access to useful resources, willingness to mobilise them, beneficial outcomes, or equal distribution.
3. **Trust as a universal proxy — L2.** Trust may be a mechanism or a result in a particular formulation, but it should not silently stand in for all forms of social capital.
4. **Closure as always good — L1/L2.** Portes explicitly requires attention to negative consequences. Closure may support coordination and support while also creating exclusion, excessive claims, or downward pressures; examine both possibilities ([Annual Reviews](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev.soc.24.1.1)).
5. **Conflating level of analysis — L1/L2.** Portes notes movement from individual asset to community/national feature. Do not infer a community-level property from individual responses, or individual access from an aggregate score, without a justified bridge ([Annual Reviews](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev.soc.24.1.1)).
6. **Causal overclaim — L2.** A relationship between network position and outcome may reflect prior advantage, institutional selection, homophily, or unobserved resources. State competing explanations and design limits.
7. **Ignoring power and convertibility — L1/L2.** In Bourdieu's formulation, relationships have value in relation to group membership, other capital, and social recognition. Do not use Bourdieu's name for a decontextualised support scale ([Illinois/Greenwood record](https://publish.illinois.edu/crittheory/files/2023/01/Bourdieu-The-Forms-of-Capital.pdf)).

### B8. From concepts to research design

The following is **L3 guidance**, not a universal operationalisation.

| Concept / mechanism | Possible analysis dimension | Possible materials | Claim boundary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Tie / membership | tie type, duration, direction, setting, membership criteria | name-generator interviews, rosters, correspondence, participation records | A named tie is not proof of support or resource transfer. |
| Resource access | information, advice, referral, material support, recognition, opportunity | episode interviews, document trails, observed referrals, administrative records | Distinguish reported availability from verified access where feasible. |
| Mobilisation | request, offer, exchange, obligation, refusal, timing | event narratives, messages/records where ethical, process tracing | Do not assume an accessible resource was used. |
| Network position | closure, bridging, brokerage, centrality, boundary crossing | bounded network data, affiliation data, organisational maps | A metric has no social-capital meaning without a proposed resource mechanism. |
| Trust / norms / sanctions | expectations, reciprocity, enforcement, risk, shared rule | interviews from multiple positions, meeting records, observation | Do not infer collective norms from one respondent. |
| Inequality / exclusion | unequal access, membership gatekeeping, costs, claims, who is left out | comparison across positions, admissions/selection records, counter-cases | Do not report only positive benefits. |

**L3 data suggestions.** Begin with an explicit unit: individual, dyad, network, organisation, community, or institutional system. For interview work, collect concrete episodes: who was approached, what resource was sought/offered, why that tie was available, what obligation or risk existed, and what happened. For documentary work, combine membership rules, referral or allocation records, and policy conditions with participants' accounts; documents alone rarely reveal informal access. For observation, trace actual interaction, exchange, gatekeeping, and coordination but do not equate visibility with relational importance. For network data, define the boundary, relation type, missing-tie strategy, and ethical protections before calculating metrics. Use negative or non-access cases to avoid treating the network as a uniformly beneficial asset.

**L3 chapter use.** In a literature review, compare the chosen definition with at least one alternative and justify the unit. In the framework, specify resource, relation, mechanism, expected boundary conditions, and plausible rival explanations. In methods, define network boundary, measurement level, access/mobilisation distinction, missingness, confidentiality, and any causal limits. In findings, present both access and non-access cases, and distinguish resource flow from outcome. In discussion, identify whether evidence supports a relation-enabled mechanism, association, selection process, or contextual condition.

### B9. Layered reading path

1. **Level 1 — orientation:** Coleman (1988) for social structure and action ([Oxford Academic](https://doi.org/10.1093/oso/9780195159509.003.0007)); Bourdieu (1986) for social capital in a wider capital framework ([Illinois/Greenwood record](https://publish.illinois.edu/crittheory/files/2023/01/Bourdieu-The-Forms-of-Capital.pdf)).
2. **Level 2 — differentiation and critique:** Lin (2001) for resources accessed through connections and relations ([Cambridge](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815447)); Portes (1998) for origins, functions, negative consequences, and conceptual stretch ([Annual Reviews](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev.soc.24.1.1)).
3. **Level 3 — collective / policy context:** Woolcock (1998) for a theoretical-synthesis and policy-framework route when the question genuinely concerns development or institutional context ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A%3A1006884930135)); revisit Bourdieu's later capital lectures for field-specific value and symbolic profit ([Wiley](https://www.wiley-vch.de/en/areas-interest/humanities-social-sciences/forms-of-capital-978-1-5095-2670-3)).

## D. C3 content-review stop conditions

Do not approve either page if it:

- calls Bourdieu's habitus a fixed destiny or equates an institution with a field without relational evidence;
- calls any relationship, trust score, or contact count “social capital” without stating the tradition, resource, mechanism, and unit;
- presents social capital as uniformly positive or ignores exclusion and unequal access;
- claims a network pattern proves causal benefit without addressing selection, prior advantage, homophily, or design limits;
- copies generic data-collection language between the two pages without a theory-specific analytic mechanism;
- presents L3 suggestions as required methods or guarantees; or
- omits the L1/L2/L3 labels and at least three independent authoritative source records per page.
