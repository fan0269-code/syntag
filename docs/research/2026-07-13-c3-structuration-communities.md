The official [Cambridge chapter record](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/realist-social-theory/realism-and-morphogenesis/9519E0707622DA9A324D8D10B1490191) gives the book’s DOI and author record; its publisher summary frames structure and agency as distinct, emergent and causally efficacious levels. This is an alternative, not a neutral confirmation of Giddens.
5. Healy, Kieran (1998), “Conceptualising Constraint: Mouzelis, Archer and the Concept of Social Structure,” *Sociology*, 32(3), 509–522, DOI [10.1177/0038038598032003006](https://doi.org/10.1177/0038038598032003006). The journal abstract distinguishes an internal reconstruction of Giddens from Archer’s external alternative and evaluates the structure–agency problem.
6. Ashley, Laura Day (2010), “The use of structuration theory to conceptualize alternative practice in education,” *British Journal of Sociology of Education*, 31(3), 337–351, DOI [10.1080/01425691003700599](https://doi.org/10.1080/01425691003700599). The journal abstract documents an educational empirical application using social system, structure, agency, constraint, ontological security, and rules/resources.

## 2. Structuration Theory

### Definition and core question

**L1 source fact.** Giddens develops structuration as a social-theoretical account of knowledgeable actors, social systems, institutions, power, and time-space rather than a theory that gives prior explanatory status to either individual action or abstract structure ([1979 UC Press](https://www.ucpress.edu/books/central-problems-in-social-theory/paper); [1984 UC Press](https://www.ucpress.edu/book/9780520057289/the-constitution-of-society)).

**L2 page-ready wording.** *Structuration Theory asks how recurrent social practices both draw upon and reproduce or modify rules and resources across time and space. It treats structure and agency as a duality: actors’ situated conduct is enabled and constrained by structural properties, while that conduct is also the medium through which those properties persist or change.* This is a concise editorial synthesis, not a claim that every action changes structure.

### Origins, scholars, and lineage

- **Formation (1970s) — L1:** *Central Problems* (1979) develops the theory in dialogue with interpretive sociology, functionalism, and structuralism, with explicit attention to action, structure, contradiction, power, social transformation, and time-space.
- **Systematization (1984) — L1:** *The Constitution of Society* presents the integrated formulation and links abstract theory to empirical social-science method ([UC Press](https://www.ucpress.edu/book/9780520057289/the-constitution-of-society)).
- **Reformulation and debate (1990s onward) — L1:** Sewell reformulates duality and transformation; Archer’s morphogenetic approach and Healy’s analysis make the structure–agency debate and the status of constraint explicit ([Sewell DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/229967); [Healy DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/0038038598032003006)).

**L2 lineage note.** Giddens is the theory’s principal author. Structuralism, functionalism, interpretive sociology, Marxism, and phenomenological/interactionist questions are intellectual interlocutors, not interchangeable ancestors. Practice Theory and Morphogenetic Theory are adjacent developments, not subtypes of Structuration Theory.

### Six core concepts and relationship

1. **Structure as rules and resources — L1:** structural properties are implicated in what practices make possible and constrain; do not reify “structure” as a separate actor ([Giddens 1984](https://www.ucpress.edu/book/9780520057289/the-constitution-of-society)).
2. **Duality of structure — L1:** structures are simultaneously the medium and outcome of recursively organized practices; this is not a simple two-way causal-variable model.
3. **Knowledgeable agency and reflexive monitoring — L1:** actors can monitor and give accounts of much of what they do, while practical consciousness and unintended consequences limit transparent self-knowledge.
4. **Power and resources — L1:** agency entails the capacity to make a difference; resources connect action to relations of domination and enablement, rather than denoting only material assets.
5. **Social systems and routinized practices — L1:** systems are reproduced through regular social practices, not identical with the virtual structural properties drawn upon in action.
6. **Time-space and institutionalization — L1:** practices, encounters, and institutions are organized across time and space; an account of change must specify those settings rather than invoke “context” generically.

**L2 relationship model.** In a given time-space setting, actors reflexively enact practices by drawing on available rules and resources. Repeated enactments reproduce a social system; altered enactments, uneven resource access, contradiction, or new coordination can contribute to transformation. Treat this as a researchable process to specify, not an automatic feedback loop.

### Candidate and alternative theory comparison

| Comparison | Shared concern | Material difference | When it may fit better |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Main candidate: Practice Theory (Bourdieu)** | Both reject a simple individual/structure split and analyse socially organized practice. | Bourdieu foregrounds habitus, fields, capitals, and symbolic power; Structuration foregrounds rules/resources, reflexive conduct, duality, and time-space recursion. Sewell explicitly puts both traditions into dialogue ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/229967)). | Choose Bourdieu where durable dispositions, unequal capital, field position, or symbolic domination are the proposed mechanisms. |
| **Alternative: Morphogenetic Theory (Archer)** | Both address structure, agency, constraint, and social change. | Archer analytically separates pre-existing structures/culture, interaction, and later elaboration; Giddens stresses their duality in ongoing practice. This is a genuine theoretical disagreement, not a vocabulary swap ([Cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/realist-social-theory/realism-and-morphogenesis/9519E0707622DA9A324D8D10B1490191); [Healy](https://doi.org/10.1177/0038038598032003006)). | Choose Morphogenetic Theory where temporal sequencing and autonomous structural conditioning must be central analytic claims. |

### Fit, boundary, and misuse

**L2 good-fit questions.** Use Structuration Theory where the research asks how participants enact, sustain, negotiate, or alter rules/resources in an organization, school, profession, policy implementation setting, or technology-mediated practice; how power and routines connect everyday conduct to institutional reproduction; or why apparently contradictory practices persist. Ashley’s education study is an example of application, not proof of universal suitability ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/01425691003700599)).

**L2 usually weak as the primary lens.** It is a poor primary fit for (a) estimating a one-off intervention effect without analysing practices or rules/resources; (b) explaining durable inequality chiefly through capital, disposition, or field position without a recursion question; (c) treating formal policy text alone as the whole structure; (d) individual attitude studies with no social-practice or institutional connection; or (e) projects that need separately measured structural causal effects before action occurs—where a morphogenetic or other realist frame may be clearer.

**L1/L2 critiques and common misuse.**

1. **L1 critique:** Archer and subsequent debate question whether duality makes pre-existing structural constraint analytically difficult to isolate ([Archer](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/realist-social-theory/realism-and-morphogenesis/9519E0707622DA9A324D8D10B1490191); [Healy](https://doi.org/10.1177/0038038598032003006)).
2. **L1 critique:** Sewell’s reformulation indicates that transformation and the relation of schemas/resources require further specification ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/229967)).
3. **L2 misuse:** do not call every reciprocal influence “duality of structure”; name the actual rule, resource, practice, temporal setting, and evidence of reproduction or alteration.
4. **L2 misuse:** do not equate “structure” with a policy, organization chart, or demographic category, nor “agency” with unconstrained individual choice.
5. **L2 boundary:** do not infer institutional transformation merely because an actor resisted or narrated a different intention; trace whether practices, rules, resources, or coordination changed.

### From concepts to research design

**L3, select only what fits the question.** The analytic unit can be a recurrent practice in a defined time-space setting, examined through actors and the rules/resources they draw upon—not “the whole organization” by default.

| Concept | Possible analytic question | Possible material | Claim limit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rules/resources | What formal, informal, material, or authoritative resources are drawn on? | policy texts, meeting records, budgets, platforms, interviews | Documents show codification, not automatically enacted rules. |
| Reflexive agency | How do participants describe monitoring, justification, alternatives, and constraints? | episode interviews, reflective logs, observation | Accounts are evidence of practical knowledge, not transparent motives. |
| Routine/system reproduction | Which practices recur and with what effects? | repeated observation, workflow traces, archival sequences | Repetition alone does not prove a structural mechanism. |
| Power/domination | Who can authorize, allocate, sanction, or redefine practice? | decision records, resource flows, interaction data | Do not reduce power to formal rank. |
| Time-space | How do sites, schedules, encounters, and histories organize practice? | calendars, spatial layouts, policy chronology, fieldnotes | Context needs a specified link to practice. |

**L3 data and analysis suggestions.** Combine interviews about concrete episodes with observation of current practice and documents that show rules/resources; compare routine and exceptional cases; build a chronology before claiming transformation; actively seek cases where actors drew on the same rule/resource differently. These are options requiring ethical, access, and disciplinary judgement, not method prescriptions.

**L3 chapter use.** In the literature review, distinguish duality from a generic “structure versus agency” slogan. In the theoretical framework, state the practices, rules/resources, power relation, and time-space scope. In methods, justify how material speaks to enactment rather than merely formal design. In findings, show episodes and recurrence. In discussion, separate reproduction, local adaptation, and evidence of structural change.

## 3. Communities of Practice

### Definition and core question

**L1 source fact.** Lave and Wenger frame learning as situated participation and introduce LPP to analyse relations among newcomers, old-timers, activities, identities, artefacts, and communities of practice ([Cambridge/DOI](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815355)). Wenger develops a social-participation theory linking practice, community, meaning, and identity ([Cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/communities-of-practice/724C22A03B12D11DFC345EEF0AD3F22A)).

**L2 page-ready wording.** *Communities of Practice is a social learning theory that examines how people develop competence, meaning, and identity through sustained participation in a shared practice. A CoP is not any group with a common label: evidence should show mutual engagement, a jointly negotiated enterprise, and a shared repertoire of resources and ways of doing.*

### Origins, scholars, and lineage

- **Situated-learning origin (1991) — L1:** Lave and Wenger’s *Situated Learning* introduced LPP through apprenticeship-oriented cases; it should not be reduced to a universal onboarding sequence ([Cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/highereducation/books/situated-learning/6915ABD21C8E4619F750A4D4ACA616CD)).
- **Social-participation formulation (1998) — L1:** Wenger’s *Communities of Practice* broadens the account to community, practice, meaning, identity, boundaries, and organizational/educational design ([Cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/communities-of-practice/724C22A03B12D11DFC345EEF0AD3F22A)).
- **Critical and empirical diversification (2000s onward) — L1:** Contu and Willmott foreground power; Cox finds divergent concepts across seminal texts; Barton and Tusting develop language, power, and social-context questions; Eberle and colleagues test access conditions in one concrete setting ([INFORMS](https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.14.3.283.15167); [SAGE](https://doi.org/10.1177/0165551505057016)).

**L2 lineage note.** CoP emerged from situated learning and social participation. It is adjacent to sociocultural learning, activity theory, organizational learning, and practice traditions, but “community of practice” is not a generic synonym for collaboration, professional development, social network, or online forum.

### Six core concepts and relationship

1. **Practice — L1:** the shared, historically developed doing through which meaning and competence are negotiated; practice includes but exceeds task lists.
2. **Mutual engagement — L1:** sustained participation and relations among people involved in the practice; mere co-location or membership is insufficient.
3. **Joint enterprise — L1:** what members negotiate as worth pursuing and for which they develop mutual accountability; it need not mean everyone has identical goals.
4. **Shared repertoire — L1:** communal resources such as routines, terms, stories, tools, documents, genres, and symbols developed or adopted over time.
5. **Participation and reification — L1:** meaning is negotiated through engagement and through objects, descriptions, tools, categories, and other reified forms; avoid treating documents as self-explanatory.
6. **Identity trajectory / LPP — L1/L2:** participation can shape who counts as competent and belongs. LPP is especially useful for newcomers’ access to meaningful practice; it should not imply that all participants move linearly from margin to centre ([Lave & Wenger](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815355); [Eberle et al.](https://doi.org/10.1080/10508406.2014.883978)).

**L2 relationship model.** A shared enterprise brings people into ongoing engagement; through that engagement they negotiate meanings and create/use a repertoire; participation and reification make competence visible and contestable; participants’ identities and access to consequential tasks change with their trajectories. Power, exclusion, language, and organizational conditions can shape every link in this model.

### Candidate and alternative theory comparison

| Comparison | Shared concern | Material difference | When it may fit better |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Main candidate: Activity Theory** | Both view learning as social, mediated, and embedded in collective activity. | CoP centres participation, identity, belonging, and practice coherence; Activity Theory centres an activity system, object, mediating artefacts, rules, division of labour, contradictions, and expansive change. | Choose Activity Theory when the primary unit is a multi-role activity system and its object/contradictions rather than a community’s participation trajectory. |
| **Alternative: Social Network Analysis** | Both can study ties, knowledge circulation, access, and boundary crossing. | CoP requires an interpretive account of practice, mutual engagement, enterprise, and repertoire; network analysis models relational pattern and position, which may exist without a CoP. | Choose network analysis when tie structure, brokerage, density, or diffusion is the main explanatory object. |

### Fit, boundary, and misuse

**L2 good-fit questions.** Use CoP where the question concerns how teachers, students, practitioners, or cross-role participants learn to participate in an enduring practice; how newcomers gain or are denied access to consequential tasks; how repertoires and mutual accountability develop; how identity/competence are negotiated; or how practice crosses and strains boundaries.

**L2 usually weak as the primary lens.** It is weak where (a) a short-lived project team has no evidence of sustained practice; (b) the question is only about information transfer or interpersonal ties; (c) formal training outcomes are measured without participation/identity/practice data; (d) hierarchy, coercion, or exclusion is the central mechanism but power is not analytically developed; or (e) a website, chat group, or branded professional-learning programme is called a CoP solely because it has members.

**L1/L2 critiques and common misuse.**

1. **L1 critique:** Cox shows that influential CoP texts differ in their constructions of learning, power, change, diversity, informality, community, and practice; do not present a single settled version ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/0165551505057016)).
2. **L1 critique:** Contu and Willmott argue that power relations require re-embedding in situated-learning theory ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.14.3.283.15167)).
3. **L1 critique:** language, reification, and wider social context may be under-analysed if the local group is treated as self-contained ([Cambridge excerpt](https://assets.cambridge.org/052183/6433/excerpt/0521836433_excerpt.htm)).
4. **L2 misuse:** never diagnose a CoP from shared interest, a mailing list, or formal organizational designation alone; show all three dimensions and the practice itself.
5. **L2 misuse:** do not equate LPP with a universal novice-to-expert ladder, passive observation, or harmonious inclusion. Examine legitimacy, access, contribution, recognition, and exclusion.
6. **L2 boundary:** do not assume CoP participation produces learning, equity, innovation, or organizational benefit; those are empirical questions under particular material and power conditions.

### From concepts to research design

**L3, select only what fits the question.** The primary analytic unit is a socially sustained practice and its participants, not merely an organization or a named group.

| Concept | Possible analytic question | Possible material | Claim limit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mutual engagement | Who engages around consequential practice, with what regularity and recognition? | observation, interaction records, participant accounts | Attendance does not establish engagement. |
| Joint enterprise | What is negotiated as the work, problem, responsibility, or standard? | meeting talk, decisions, disagreements, task traces | Formal objectives are not automatically a joint enterprise. |
| Shared repertoire | Which tools, routines, stories, genres, and terms carry practice? | artefacts, templates, transcripts, fieldnotes | A common document is not itself a repertoire. |
| LPP / identity trajectory | What access do newcomers have, and how do roles/recognition change? | longitudinal interviews, task allocation, mentoring episodes | Do not infer learning or belonging from tenure alone. |
| Boundaries and power | Who may define competence, authorize participation, or withhold access? | gatekeeping events, resource allocation, silences, cross-group encounters | “Community” must not conceal hierarchy or exclusion. |

**L3 data and analysis suggestions.** Observe real work and interaction where ethically possible; collect locally meaningful artefacts and versions of them; interview both newer and established participants around concrete episodes; follow an entry, access, or boundary-crossing process over time; look for non-participation, departure, and disagreement as well as cohesion. Eberle et al. illustrate that recruitment and access conditions can be studied in a specified setting, but their findings should not be generalized as a CoP checklist ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/10508406.2014.883978)).

**L3 chapter use.** In the literature review, distinguish CoP from generic groups, networks, and formal programmes. In the framework, specify which dimensions and power/boundary assumptions matter. In methods, explain why the evidence reaches practice rather than only attitudes. In findings, trace engagement, enterprise, repertoire, and trajectories with countercases. In discussion, state whether a CoP was evidenced, partial, contested, or absent; do not use the theory as a success label.

## 5. Content-review stop conditions

Do not approve either D2 page if it:

- calls Structuration Theory a simple balance or measurable two-way causal variable between “structure” and “agency”;
- collapses Giddens, Bourdieu, and Archer into one theory;
- treats a policy or organization chart as the complete meaning of structure;
- calls any team, platform, or professional development programme a Community of Practice without evidence of practice, mutual engagement, joint enterprise, and repertoire;
- presents LPP as a required linear movement from novice to expert;
- hides hierarchy, language, exclusion, or power behind the word “community”;
- presents L3 data/design suggestions as generally valid methodological conclusions; or
- lacks the L1/L2/L3 distinction and a traceable layered reading path.
