## 1. Publication rule and audit result

### 1.1 Candidate rule

A C5 Work may be published only if it has all of the following:

1. a stable L1 bibliographic/access record;
2. a bounded central question and contribution that do not overstate what the work establishes;
3. a real relation to one or more of the existing twelve theory pages;
4. a legal reader route (DOI resolver, publisher, university-hosted record, journal, library, or authoritative institution); and
5. at least one non-duplicative concept relation.

The list below contains **19 candidates**. It intentionally keeps separate traditions separate: an authoritative UNESCO report is an institutional reference work, not a canonical single theory of educational equity; teacher life history is a research tradition; and Teacher Professional Development is a plural field rather than one settled theory.

## 3. Twenty-four deduplicated core-concept candidates

**Editorial entity rule (L2).** A concept becomes one page only when the definition can stay stable while its theory-specific meaning is shown explicitly. The `Related` field identifies theory/page and candidate Work relations; a semicolon separates an L1 anchored relation from an L2 cross-theory distinction. Each `Observable` field is **L3** and should be presented as an example of what might be documented, not a measurement recipe.

### 3.1 Life Course Theory

1. **Trajectory** — **Definition (L2):** a longer-term pathway through roles, statuses, or domains, distinct from one bounded event. **Related:** Life Course Theory; *The Life Course as Developmental Theory*. It differs from a teacher life history because a trajectory is an analytic temporal pattern, whereas life-history research concerns interpreted narration. **Observable (L3):** dated role/status sequences, durations, and contextual records. **Misuse:** treating any chronological list as a trajectory or claiming causal direction from order alone.
2. **Transition** — **Definition (L2):** a bounded change in role, status, or setting whose timing and sequence can matter for a pathway. **Related:** Life Course Theory; Elder (1998). It differs from a turning point: a transition need not redirect a subsequent trajectory. **Observable (L3):** entry/exit dates, role changes, participant accounts of conditions. **Misuse:** calling every reported event a turning point.
3. **Turning point** — **Definition (L2):** a transition supported by evidence as redirecting a later pathway or its meaning. **Related:** Life Course Theory; Elder (1998). It is not interchangeable with a Multiple Streams policy window, which is an agenda-setting opportunity rather than biographical redirection. **Observable (L3):** before/after sequences, contemporaneous records, accounts of changed alternatives. **Misuse:** inferring redirection only because an event was memorable.

### 3.2 Teacher Identity Theory

4. **Teacher professional identity** — **Definition (L2):** teachers’ negotiated understanding of who they are and may become in relation to professional work, contexts, and others. **Related:** Teacher Identity Theory; Beijaard, Meijer, and Verloop (2004). It differs from habitus: identity is an explicit analytic account of professional self-relation, while habitus concerns socially shaped practical dispositions. **Observable (L3):** self-descriptions, role claims, dilemmas, interactional positioning, and documents about role expectations. **Misuse:** coding any attitude, demographic category, or stable personality trait as identity.
5. **Teacher self-understanding** — **Definition (L2):** the reflective and interpretive dimension through which a teacher makes sense of teaching self and work. **Related:** Teacher Identity Theory; Kelchtermans (2009). It can be studied through life-history narratives but does not make narration a transparent factual record. **Observable (L3):** narrative accounts, reflective writing, accounts of recognition/vulnerability. **Misuse:** treating a single interview answer as a complete or unchanging identity.

### 3.3 Structuration Theory

6. **Duality of structure** — **Definition (L2):** structures are analytically understood as both the medium and outcome of recurrent social practice. **Related:** Structuration Theory; Giddens (1984). It differs from the analytic separation used by morphogenetic alternatives and from treating structure/agency as two independent variables. **Observable (L3):** episodes in which actors draw on and reproduce, modify, or contest an arrangement. **Misuse:** a generic claim that “structure and agency interact” without identified practice.
7. **Rules and resources** — **Definition (L2):** interpretive and material means drawn on in the conduct and organisation of practice. **Related:** Structuration Theory; Giddens (1984). It differs from institutional isomorphism: a rule/resource used in practice is not itself evidence of field-level conformity pressure. **Observable (L3):** procedures, artefacts, allocations, accounts of how a rule was invoked or bypassed. **Misuse:** reading a formal rule as proof of enactment.
8. **Recursive practice** — **Definition (L2):** recurrent action through which social arrangements are sustained or altered over time. **Related:** Structuration Theory; Giddens (1984). It differs from Bourdieu’s practical logic: both concern practice, but their conceptual vocabulary and explanations of power/disposition differ. **Observable (L3):** repeated routines, deviations, repairs, and traces of changed coordination. **Misuse:** calling any repetition reproduction without comparing conditions and consequences.

### 3.4 Communities of Practice

9. **Legitimate peripheral participation** — **Definition (L2):** a situated-learning account of newcomers’ participation and access in a practice. **Related:** Communities of Practice; Lave and Wenger (1991). It differs from generic onboarding because legitimacy, participation, and practice must be evidenced. **Observable (L3):** access to tasks, mentoring, recognition, use of artefacts, and exclusion episodes. **Misuse:** assuming a linear movement from peripheral to full participation.
10. **Mutual engagement** — **Definition (L2):** sustained engagement among participants around a shared practice, not mere co-location or formal membership. **Related:** Communities of Practice; Wenger (1998). It differs from social capital: engagement may matter for learning/competence without establishing relation-enabled resource access as the primary mechanism. **Observable (L3):** recurring interaction, jointly handled problems, mutual recognition. **Misuse:** labelling all teams or networks a community of practice.
11. **Shared repertoire** — **Definition (L2):** shared routines, language, stories, tools, and other resources through which a practice is recognised and negotiated. **Related:** Communities of Practice; Wenger (1998). It differs from a structuration rule/resource because it is analysed here as a participation/meaning resource within a community of practice. **Observable (L3):** recurring terms, artefacts, routines, and contested meanings. **Misuse:** treating an organisation’s written handbook as evidence that participants share a repertoire.

### 3.5 Practice Theory (Bourdieu)

12. **Habitus** — **Definition (L2):** durable, socially shaped dispositions that orient perception, judgment, and practical action. **Related:** Practice Theory (Bourdieu); *Outline of a Theory of Practice*. It differs from teacher self-understanding and from an immutable personality trait. **Observable (L3):** patterned practical judgments across situations, biography-linked accounts, and counter-cases. **Misuse:** treating habitus as destiny or inferring it from class labels alone.
13. **Field** — **Definition (L2):** a structured arena of positions, stakes, struggles, and forms of recognition. **Related:** Practice Theory (Bourdieu); *Outline of a Theory of Practice*. It differs from an institution: not every organisation, sector, or interview setting constitutes a field. **Observable (L3):** position relations, valued resources, classification disputes, entry/exit mechanisms. **Misuse:** renaming any context a field without showing relations and stakes.
14. **Capital conversion** — **Definition (L2):** the context-dependent relation through which economic, cultural, social, or symbolic resources can be accumulated, recognised, or converted. **Related:** Practice Theory and the Bourdieu tradition of Social Capital Theory; *The Forms of Capital*. It differs from Lin’s narrower resource-access formulation because conversion depends on recognition and field relations. **Observable (L3):** credential use, referrals, recognition decisions, resource exchanges, failed conversions. **Misuse:** assuming that possession of a resource automatically has value in every field.
15. **Symbolic power** — **Definition (L2):** power exercised through socially recognised classifications, legitimacy, and meanings rather than only direct coercion. **Related:** Practice Theory; Bourdieu, “Symbolic Power” (1979) and *Outline*. It differs from organisational legitimacy: both concern recognition, but symbolic power analyses relations of classification and domination rather than only organisational appropriateness. **Observable (L3):** naming/classification practices, credential valuation, routine deference, contested authority. **Misuse:** calling any persuasive language symbolic power without a social recognition relation.

### 3.6 Social Capital Theory

16. **Relational resource access** — **Definition (L2):** access to information, support, influence, or opportunities made possible through specified social relations. **Related:** Social Capital Theory; Lin (2001), Coleman (1988), and—only when expressly named—Bourdieu (1986). It differs from mutual engagement: a relation may provide access without constituting shared practice. **Observable (L3):** concrete requests, referrals, information flows, support episodes, and non-access cases. **Misuse:** counting contacts as proof of resources or benefit.
17. **Obligation and reciprocity** — **Definition (L2):** expectations of exchange, return, or claim-making that may organise relation-enabled action in a stated social-capital formulation. **Related:** Social Capital Theory; Coleman (1988). It differs from a universally beneficial “trust” variable: obligations can impose costs and exclusions. **Observable (L3):** requests, refusals, reciprocal transfers, sanctions, reported burdens. **Misuse:** assuming reciprocity is present, equal, voluntary, or positive in all ties.

### 3.7 Teacher Professional Development

18. **Professional learning** — **Definition (L2):** ongoing learning related to teachers’ knowledge, practice, roles, and working conditions. **Related:** Teacher Professional Development entry; Day (1999), Clarke and Hollingsworth (2002). It differs from Communities of Practice because professional learning may be organised through multiple models and contexts, not only participation in a CoP. **Observable (L3):** learning opportunities, changed practices, mediating conditions, and counterexamples. **Misuse:** treating attendance, satisfaction, or a single workshop as evidence of development or learner outcomes.

### 3.8 Teacher Life History Research

19. **Life history** — **Definition (L2):** an interpretive inquiry into narrated lives in social and historical context. **Related:** Teacher Life History Research; Goodson (2013). It differs from a life-course trajectory: it foregrounds personal representation and interpretation rather than supplying a complete explanatory theory of pathways. **Observable (L3):** narrated biography, chronology prompts, contextual documents, reflexive notes, with ethics safeguards. **Misuse:** treating personal narrative as an unmediated factual chronology or population estimate.

### 3.9 Educational Equity

20. **Educational equity** — **Definition (L2):** an explicit normative and empirical inquiry into avoidable inequalities in educational access, participation, treatment, resources, recognition, or outcomes. **Related:** Educational Equity entry; UNESCO (2020) for institutional context and Sen (1992) for one normative route. It differs from social capital and Bourdieu because those may explain unequal access/advantage but do not, on their own, state the equity standard. **Observable (L3):** population/comparator definition, access/participation records, allocation rules, participant accounts, outcome distributions. **Misuse:** using “equity” as a slogan without a stated dimension, comparison, normative basis, and mechanism.

### 3.10 Institutional Theory

21. **Institutional isomorphism** — **Definition (L2):** processes through which organisations in a field become more similar under specified institutional pressures. **Related:** Institutional Theory; DiMaggio and Powell (1983). It differs from structuration’s recursive practice: isomorphism concerns field-level patterned similarity and mechanism, not every routine. **Observable (L3):** cross-organisation comparisons, policies, professional standards, accounts of pressure, timing of adoption. **Misuse:** inferring coercive, mimetic, or normative pressure solely from similarity.
22. **Decoupling** — **Definition (L2):** a possible separation between formal structures and ongoing activity in an organisational neo-institutionalist account. **Related:** Institutional Theory; Meyer and Rowan (1977). It differs from non-compliance: a document-practice difference alone does not establish the organisational relation or its function. **Observable (L3):** formal policies, observed practices, implementation records, accounts from multiple positions. **Misuse:** treating any gap or implementation difficulty as decoupling.

### 3.11 Street-Level Bureaucracy

23. **Frontline discretion** — **Definition (L2):** situated judgment exercised by frontline public-service workers while applying policy to cases under organisational conditions. **Related:** Street-Level Bureaucracy; Lipsky (2010). It differs from a general claim of worker autonomy and from Multiple Streams agenda change. **Observable (L3):** case-processing choices, rule interpretation, workload/resource conditions, coping episodes, client interaction. **Misuse:** treating discretion as misconduct, benevolence, or unconstrained personal choice without the service setting.

### 3.12 Multiple Streams Framework

24. **Streams, coupling, and policy window** — **Definition (L2):** a linked MSF vocabulary in which problem, policy, and political processes remain analytically distinct, while a temporary window can enable coupling. **Related:** Multiple Streams Framework; Kingdon (1995, 2nd ed.). It differs from a life-course turning point: both concern timing, but this concept concerns policy agenda opportunity and actor strategy, not individual pathways. **Observable (L3):** dated indicators, proposals, political events, actor accounts, and evidence of a claimed coupling. **Misuse:** assigning the three streams retrospectively after an outcome is known or using MSF as an implementation theory.

## 4. Required relationship and verification model for C5 implementation

### 4.1 Work relationships

Every published Work must have a direct `work ↔ theory` relation. Shared works require a **relation qualifier**, not an implied equivalence:

- Bourdieu (1986) ↔ Practice Theory: `core_concept_source` for capital/conversion.
- Bourdieu (1986) ↔ Social Capital: `tradition_source` for the Bourdieu formulation only.
- Wenger (1998) ↔ Communities of Practice: `core_work`; ↔ Teacher Identity: `adjacent_theory_source` only.
- UNESCO (2020) ↔ Educational Equity: `institutional_context_source`, never `theory_foundation`.
- Sen (1992) ↔ Educational Equity: `normative_resource`, never `sole_definition`.

### 4.2 Concept relationships

Every published Concept must have:

1. a one-sentence L2 definition;
2. at least one explicit theory relation and one Work relation;
3. a sentence distinguishing it from the nearest non-identical concept where relevant;
4. one L3 observable expression with an explicit non-automatic inference boundary; and
5. one misuse warning.

Do not create duplicate entities for “social capital” in Bourdieu/Coleman/Lin. Use **Relational resource access** as the concept entry and attach a `tradition` qualifier to theory/work relations; the page must then state what Bourdieu, Coleman, and Lin mean differently. Likewise, do not create generic duplicate pages for “identity,” “practice,” “structure,” “equity,” or “learning.”

### 4.3 L1/L2/L3 verification rows

## 5. Explicit non-publication list for this C5 batch

The following are deliberately **not** separate Works/Concepts in the first batch:

- Additional theories, disciplines, scholars, or concepts from Psychology or Management.
- A generic “community,” “network,” “trust,” “identity,” “institution,” “practice,” or “equity” page with no bounded theory-specific definition.
- Duplicate Bourdieu social-capital works or a duplicate title for different theory pages.
- An author biography inferred from a Work citation.
- Unverified full-text links, copied PDFs without stable source attribution, or access claims beyond the linked DOI/publisher/university/library/institution record.

## 6. Implementation handoff

The C5 implementation should use this register as the reviewable pool of **19 vetted Work candidates** and **24 vetted Concept candidates**. A candidate remains unpublished if any of its required content contract fields, relation qualifiers, or L1 record cannot be preserved in the implementation schema. This evidence pack supplies source selection and wording boundaries; it does not itself publish data or modify `src/data/seed-content.ts`.
