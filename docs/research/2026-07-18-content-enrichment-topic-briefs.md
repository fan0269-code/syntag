## 0. Evidence and SEO guardrails

### Evidence levels

- **L1 source-backed vocabulary:** The cited records can support bounded vocabulary such as professional growth, professional learning, mutual engagement, frontline discretion, policy implementation, equity, access, social capital, or field/capital. They do not prove that a theory is the best fit for a new topic.
- **L2 editorial judgment:** Every `primary`, `supporting`, and `not_recommended` theory fit below is a Syntag editorial research-design judgment. It must be presented on the future page as conditional guidance, not as a source fact or universal ranking.
- **L3 research guidance:** Data materials, chapter use, and analysis design suggestions must remain conditional on question, setting, ethics, access, and disciplinary supervision.

### SEO / user-intent constraints

- Do **not** invent or estimate search volume. SERP sampling showed academic, doctoral, policy, and practitioner-research intent signals, but no reliable traffic numbers.
- Each topic should target one clear question intent: “Which theory fits this research problem, and why not the adjacent alternatives?”
- Avoid programmatic near-duplicates. The new pages should not simply restate existing Topic or Field pages; each must name a distinct explanatory object, analysis unit, data material, and anti-fit condition.
- Keep the public scope inside Education and Sociology. Do not create Psychology, Management/OB, or generic public-policy pages in this batch.

## 1. Topic brief: `teacher-professional-learning-and-change`

### Recommended English question

**How do teachers’ professional learning experiences lead to changes in practice?**

### Search / user intent

- Likely user intent: doctoral and master’s researchers looking for a theory to connect teacher professional learning, professional development, practice change, school context, and evidence of changed teaching.
- SERP sampling returned dissertation, journal, and professional-learning results around “teacher professional learning,” “teacher change,” “professional development,” “professional growth,” “learning contexts,” and “practice change.” This indicates research-design and literature-review intent, not a simple definition-only query.
- Do not state search volume, trend, or keyword difficulty. None was verified.

### Difference from existing Topic pages

- Different from **Teachers’ professional identity during reform**: this page is about learning and practice change, not primarily professional self-understanding, agency, or vulnerability during reform.
- Different from **Communities of practice in teacher learning**: this page is broader. It can include professional learning programmes, coaching, inquiry, leadership conditions, curriculum change, and multiple teacher-change models. CoP should be only a conditional supporting route when sustained shared practice is evidenced.
- Different from the **Teacher Education & Professional Development** field page: this topic should be a question-level landing page with a more specific “learning → change in practice” decision path.

### Recommended theory pathways

All fit labels below are **L2 editorial judgments, not source facts**.

| Role | Theory | L2 editorial fit rationale | Source-bounded vocabulary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary | `teacher-professional-development-theory` | Best first route when the research question asks how teacher learning, professional development, or growth relates to changed practice across contexts. | Clarke & Hollingsworth support a named professional-growth model; Timperley et al. support professional-learning conditions and context-sensitive synthesis. |
| Supporting | `communities-of-practice` | Useful only when the learning process depends on sustained participation, mutual engagement, joint enterprise, and shared repertoire rather than a programme alone. | Wenger and Lave & Wenger support social participation and legitimate peripheral participation vocabulary. |
| Not recommended as primary | `teacher-identity-theory` | Not primary when the explanatory object is learning/change rather than how teachers interpret who they are in relation to work. It can become supporting if self-understanding is an observed mediator. | Kelchtermans/identity sources support professional self-understanding, not a complete teacher-change model. |

### Explanatory focus

- How professional learning opportunities, mediating processes, conditions, and professional contexts become connected to changed teacher practice.
- The page should ask whether the study can evidence change, not merely attendance, satisfaction, or declared learning.

### Analysis unit

- Teachers’ professional-learning processes and practice changes.
- Possible secondary units: learning opportunity, department/team, school context, programme design, leadership condition, classroom practice episode.

### Data materials

- Teacher interviews or reflective accounts that describe learning processes and changes.
- Classroom observation, lesson artefacts, planning materials, or teaching records showing practice shifts.
- Professional-learning programme documents, coaching notes, inquiry-cycle records, or leadership support materials.
- Student-work or classroom-outcome evidence only if the study design can connect it cautiously to teacher learning; do not treat it as automatic proof of professional growth.

### Strengths

- Gives doctoral users a defensible route from “professional development” to researchable mechanisms.
- Distinguishes evidence of learning/change from programme participation.
- Creates internal links to `teacher-professional-development-theory`, `communities-of-practice`, `teacher-identity-theory`, and the `professional-learning` concept.

### Limitations

- Does not prove a professional-learning activity caused student outcomes.
- Does not make every workshop, PLC, coaching session, or training programme evidence of teacher development.
- Needs a named model or mechanism before it can become a robust research design.

### Core questions

1. What kind of professional learning or development is being studied?
2. What evidence shows changed teacher practice, not only participation or satisfaction?
3. Which conditions, mediating processes, or contextual factors connect learning to change?
4. Is sustained shared practice central enough to use Communities of Practice as more than a supporting lens?
5. Is professional self-understanding a mediator, or is it a separate identity question?

### Question categories

| Category | Description | Candidate theories |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Professional growth and change | Questions about how teacher learning connects to changed knowledge, beliefs, practice, or professional action. | `teacher-professional-development-theory` |
| Participation-based learning | Questions where learning occurs through sustained shared practice, mutual engagement, and repertoire. | `communities-of-practice` |
| Professional self-understanding | Questions where change is mainly about how teachers interpret themselves and their work. | `teacher-identity-theory` |

### Selection path

1. **Name the change claim.** Prompt: What changed — belief, knowledge, classroom practice, role, collaboration, or career orientation? Routing rule: use teacher professional development when change across domains is the explanatory object.
2. **Check evidence of learning.** Prompt: What materials show learning and not only attendance? Routing rule: reject a development claim if no mediating process or practice evidence is available.
3. **Inspect social participation.** Prompt: Does the study show mutual engagement, joint enterprise, and shared repertoire? Routing rule: use CoP as supporting or primary only when these conditions are evidenced.
4. **Separate identity from learning.** Prompt: Is self-understanding the central object? Routing rule: route identity-centred questions to the existing identity topic or a supporting identity lens.

### Entry point suggestions

- Field: `teacher-education-professional-development`
- Existing topic: `teachers-professional-identity-during-reform` for contrast
- Theory: `teacher-professional-development-theory`
- Supporting theory: `communities-of-practice`
- Contrast theory: `teacher-identity-theory`
- Work: `teacher-development-clarke-hollingsworth-2002`
- Concept: `professional-learning`

### Meta intent

Help a doctoral researcher decide whether “teacher professional learning/change” should be analysed through a professional-development/growth model, a participation lens, or an identity lens.

### Thin page / duplicate risk check

- Risk: high overlap with the `Teacher Education & Professional Development` field page and the existing identity topic.
- Mitigation: make the page specifically about evidence of teacher learning leading to practice change; include anti-fit language for attendance-only or satisfaction-only studies.
- Required uniqueness test before integration: if the draft can be summarized as “teacher professional development is about professional learning,” it is too thin. It must include change claim, evidence materials, and theory-selection boundaries.

## 2. Topic brief: `education-policy-implementation-frontline-discretion`

### Recommended English question

**How do frontline educators and administrators exercise discretion when implementing education policy?**

### Search / user intent

- Likely user intent: researchers comparing education policy implementation, street-level bureaucracy, school leadership, district administration, teacher discretion, and local adaptation.
- SERP sampling returned results on frontline discretion, bottom-up implementation, school change implementation, district administrators, principal discretion, and school-level policy enactment. This suggests users are looking for implementation-stage theory, not agenda-setting theory.
- Do not state search volume, trend, or keyword difficulty.

### Difference from existing Topic pages

- Different from **Educational transitions over time**: this is not about individual or cohort pathways over time.
- Different from **Inequality in educational and social fields**: this page may address unequal implementation effects, but its primary mechanism is frontline discretion under implementation constraints, not field/capital relations.
- Different from the **Educational Equity & Policy** field page: this page should isolate the post-adoption implementation stage and distinguish it from agenda setting.
- It complements but should not duplicate any future comparison page like “Multiple Streams vs Street-Level Bureaucracy.”

### Recommended theory pathways

All fit labels below are **L2 editorial judgments, not source facts**.

| Role | Theory | L2 editorial fit rationale | Source-bounded vocabulary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary | `street-level-bureaucracy` | Best first route when the question is about how policy is interpreted, adapted, rationed, or enacted by frontline workers under organisational constraints. | Lipsky sources support frontline public-service discretion and implementation conditions. |
| Supporting | `institutional-theory` | Useful when formal policy adoption, legitimacy, organisational field pressure, or decoupling help explain why implementation differs from the official rule. | Meyer & Rowan and DiMaggio & Powell support institutional rules, legitimacy, decoupling, and isomorphism vocabulary. |
| Not recommended as primary | `multiple-streams-framework` | Not primary when the policy has already been adopted and the research object is delivery/enactment. It can support a separate pre-adoption agenda-setting section if the study traces both stages. | Kingdon/Zahariadis sources support agenda setting, alternatives, ambiguity, and policy windows, not everyday frontline delivery. |

### Explanatory focus

- How local educators, principals, district administrators, counsellors, or other frontline actors interpret policy rules, allocate limited resources, manage demands, and produce delivered policy.
- The page should treat discretion as situated judgment under constraints, not as misconduct, benevolence, or pure autonomy.

### Analysis unit

- Frontline implementation episode, case-processing decision, school-level enactment, district/school boundary-spanning practice, or actor-policy interaction.
- Possible secondary units: policy text, implementation guidance, workload condition, accountability requirement, client/student/family interaction.

### Data materials

- Policy texts, guidance documents, implementation plans, circulars, accountability frameworks.
- Interviews with teachers, principals, district administrators, support staff, students, families, or policymakers.
- Case files, meeting minutes, decision records, referral records, resource allocation documents, observation of implementation practices.
- Timeline materials if the study also examines agenda setting; keep that stage analytically separate.

### Strengths

- Gives users a strong route for education policy questions after formal adoption.
- Creates a clear contrast between Street-Level Bureaucracy and Multiple Streams Framework.
- Can support equity-sensitive implementation studies without making equity itself the causal mechanism.

### Limitations

- Does not explain why an issue reached the policy agenda unless paired with a policy-process framework.
- Does not automatically condemn or celebrate discretion.
- Requires evidence of frontline interaction, consequential judgment, and organisational constraints; generic “implementation challenges” are insufficient.

### Core questions

1. What policy has already been adopted, and what implementation site is being studied?
2. Who is the frontline actor, and what discretion do they exercise?
3. What constraints shape the discretion: workload, resources, ambiguity, accountability, client interaction, or organisational rules?
4. Does institutional legitimacy or decoupling help explain the gap between formal policy and enacted practice?
5. Is agenda setting part of the study, or should Multiple Streams be excluded from the primary route?

### Question categories

| Category | Description | Candidate theories |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Frontline delivery | Case-level or school-level enactment under constraints. | `street-level-bureaucracy` |
| Formal policy and legitimacy | Organisational adoption, decoupling, legitimacy, field pressure. | `institutional-theory` |
| Agenda and policy choice | Pre-adoption issue attention, proposals, politics, windows. | `multiple-streams-framework` |

### Selection path

1. **Locate the policy stage.** Prompt: Is the question about agenda formation, formal adoption, or frontline delivery? Routing rule: use Street-Level Bureaucracy only for implementation/enactment; use MSF only for agenda/choice.
2. **Name the frontline actor.** Prompt: Which actor makes consequential judgments in direct or near-direct service delivery? Routing rule: reject SLB if no frontline discretion or client/service interaction is evidenced.
3. **Specify constraints.** Prompt: What resources, workload, rules, ambiguity, or accountability pressures shape the decision? Routing rule: discretion must be contextualized, not individualized.
4. **Check institutional mechanisms.** Prompt: Is the gap between policy and practice about legitimacy, formal structure, or decoupling? Routing rule: add Institutional Theory only when formal/institutional evidence exists.

### Entry point suggestions

- Field: `educational-equity-policy`
- Theory: `street-level-bureaucracy`
- Supporting theory: `institutional-theory`
- Contrast theory: `multiple-streams-framework`
- Work: `lipsky-2010-street-level-bureaucracy`
- Concept: `frontline-discretion`
- Potential external source to add after verification: Honig 2006 on frontline district central-office administrators in education policy implementation.

### Meta intent

Help researchers avoid using a generic “policy implementation” label by deciding whether their question is about frontline discretion, institutional adoption/decoupling, or agenda setting.

### Thin page / duplicate risk check

- Risk: medium overlap with `educational-equity-policy` and any future MSF/SLB comparison page.
- Mitigation: title and H1 should foreground **frontline discretion** and **implementation**, not broad “education policy.”
- Required uniqueness test before integration: the page must include a post-adoption policy stage, named frontline actor, discretion condition, and implementation materials.

## 3. Topic brief: `access-to-educational-support-and-opportunity`

### Recommended English question

**How do students gain or lose access to educational support and opportunity?**

### Search / user intent

- Likely user intent: researchers examining unequal access to educational opportunity, support services, rigorous coursework, quality teaching, resources, inclusion, disadvantage, and opportunity gaps.
- SERP sampling returned policy and research results about unequal access to educational opportunity, educational resources, college/career pathways, support, and equity. This indicates a practical research-design intent around access/opportunity rather than a purely conceptual “inequality” query.
- Do not state search volume, trend, or keyword difficulty.

### Difference from existing Topic pages

- Different from **Inequality in educational and social fields**: this page starts with access to support/opportunity and an explicit comparator, not with Bourdieu’s field position, habitus, capital conversion, and symbolic recognition.
- Different from **Educational transitions over time**: transitions may matter, but this page is about access conditions and support mechanisms rather than temporal pathways.
- Different from **Education Policy Implementation and Frontline Discretion**: frontline implementation can be a supporting mechanism only when the access issue turns on policy delivery.
- Different from the **Educational Equity & Policy** field page: this is a specific Topic page for support/opportunity access, not the whole equity-policy field.

### Recommended theory pathways

All fit labels below are **L2 editorial judgments, not source facts**.

| Role | Theory | L2 editorial fit rationale | Source-bounded vocabulary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary | `educational-equity-theory` | Best first route when the question asks who has access to support/opportunity, compared with whom, on what dimension, and why that difference matters normatively. | UNESCO and OECD sources support bounded inclusion/equity policy context, not a universal equity metric. |
| Supporting | `social-capital-theory` | Useful when access depends on relationships, referrals, information, trust, institutional gatekeeping, or resource mobilisation through ties. | Coleman and related social-capital sources support relation-enabled resource vocabulary. |
| Not recommended as primary | `practice-theory-bourdieu` | Not primary if the study does not examine field position, capital value/conversion, habitus, classification, or symbolic recognition. It can become supporting if opportunity is explicitly field-conditioned. | Bourdieu sources support field/capital vocabulary, not every access question. |

### Explanatory focus

- How educational support and opportunity become available, blocked, distributed, recognised, or mobilised for particular students or groups.
- The page should force the user to name the support/opportunity type, population, comparator, and mechanism.

### Analysis unit

- Student/group access to a specified support or opportunity.
- Possible secondary units: school programme, course placement, counselling/support process, family-school relation, referral pathway, resource allocation, institutional rule.

### Data materials

- Access, allocation, placement, referral, participation, support-use, and outcome records.
- Interviews or focus groups with students, families, teachers, counsellors, support staff, or administrators.
- Programme eligibility rules, guidance documents, resource allocation policies, application processes, communications.
- Network or relational evidence when Social Capital is used.
- Field/capital evidence only if Bourdieu is used as a supporting lens.

### Strengths

- Matches likely user questions around opportunity gaps and support access more directly than a broad inequality page.
- Helps separate normative equity framing from explanatory mechanisms.
- Builds internal bridges among `educational-equity-theory`, `social-capital-theory`, `practice-theory-bourdieu`, and `street-level-bureaucracy` without collapsing them.

### Limitations

- Educational Equity does not by itself identify a causal mechanism.
- Social Capital should not be reduced to tie counts or “knowing people.”
- Bourdieu should not be invoked unless field/capital evidence is actually available.
- Opportunity must be specified; generic “students lack opportunity” is too vague.

### Core questions

1. What specific support or opportunity is at issue?
2. Which students or groups are compared, and on what normative basis?
3. What mechanism shapes access: allocation rule, relational resource, frontline discretion, field/capital, recognition, information, or gatekeeping?
4. What evidence distinguishes access from participation, benefit, or outcome?
5. What would make the selected theory a poor fit?

### Question categories

| Category | Description | Candidate theories |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Equity and opportunity | Access, distribution, participation, recognition, support, and outcome with an explicit comparator. | `educational-equity-theory` |
| Relational resource access | Information, referrals, trust, advocacy, or support flows through specified ties. | `social-capital-theory` |
| Field-conditioned advantage | Opportunity shaped by capital value, field position, classification, or recognition. | `practice-theory-bourdieu` |
| Policy delivery access | Access shaped by frontline interpretation or discretionary implementation. | `street-level-bureaucracy` |

### Selection path

1. **Specify the opportunity.** Prompt: Is the issue coursework, support service, counselling, teacher quality, funding, participation, recognition, or pathway access? Routing rule: do not publish if the opportunity is unnamed.
2. **Define the comparator.** Prompt: Who is compared with whom, and why is the difference educationally significant? Routing rule: use Educational Equity only after the normative/comparative basis is explicit.
3. **Choose the mechanism.** Prompt: Is access shaped by policy allocation, relations, field/capital, or frontline discretion? Routing rule: keep the equity frame separate from the explanatory theory.
4. **Check evidence level.** Prompt: What materials can show access, non-access, gatekeeping, mobilisation, or recognition? Routing rule: reject unsupported causal language.

### Entry point suggestions

- Field: `educational-equity-policy`
- Field: `sociology-of-education`
- Existing topic: `inequality-in-educational-and-social-fields` for contrast
- Theory: `educational-equity-theory`
- Supporting theory: `social-capital-theory`
- Conditional supporting theory: `street-level-bureaucracy`
- Contrast theory: `practice-theory-bourdieu`
- Concept: `educational-equity`
- Concept: `relational-resource-access`

### Meta intent

Help researchers turn a broad opportunity-gap question into a defensible theory choice by separating equity framing, relational resource access, and field/capital explanations.

### Thin page / duplicate risk check

- Risk: high overlap with `inequality-in-educational-and-social-fields` and the `Educational Equity & Policy` field page.
- Mitigation: anchor the page in **access to specific support/opportunity** and require a support/opportunity type plus comparator.
- Required uniqueness test before integration: if the page does not name support/opportunity materials and an access mechanism, it should be folded into the equity field page instead.

## 4. Topic brief: `communities-of-practice-in-teacher-learning`

### Recommended English question

**When is Communities of Practice a good fit for studying teacher learning?**

### Search / user intent

- Likely user intent: researchers asking whether communities of practice, PLCs, teacher communities, co-planning, online teacher communities, or collaborative professional learning should be analysed through CoP.
- SERP sampling returned teacher-learning-in-CoP studies, co-planning, online teacher communities, professional learning groups, systematic reviews, and teacher education CoP results. This indicates comparison and fit intent: users need to know when “community” is analytically sufficient and when it is only a programme label.
- Do not state search volume, trend, or keyword difficulty.

### Difference from existing Topic pages

- Different from **Teacher professional learning and change**: this page is narrower and should make CoP primary only when social participation in shared practice is the mechanism.
- Different from **Teachers’ professional identity during reform**: this page is about learning through participation; identity may appear in CoP, but teacher professional identity is not the primary decision route unless self-understanding is central.
- Different from **Inequality in educational and social fields**: CoP is not primary for field/capital inequality questions.
- Different from a generic “professional learning community” page: it must test whether the empirical case meets CoP conditions rather than equating any PLC label with CoP.

### Recommended theory pathways

All fit labels below are **L2 editorial judgments, not source facts**.

| Role | Theory | L2 editorial fit rationale | Source-bounded vocabulary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary | `communities-of-practice` | Best first route when teacher learning is explained through sustained participation, mutual engagement, joint enterprise, shared repertoire, boundary work, or legitimate peripheral participation. | Wenger and Lave & Wenger support CoP and LPP vocabulary; Cox supports conceptual caution. |
| Supporting | `teacher-professional-development-theory` | Useful for translating participation into professional growth, changed practice, and teacher-learning outcomes. | Clarke & Hollingsworth and Timperley support professional-learning and growth vocabulary. |
| Not recommended as primary | `social-capital-theory` | Not primary when the question is about shared practice and learning rather than resource access through ties. It may support a separate access-to-support question. | Coleman supports relation-enabled resources, not the full CoP practice/community/identity account. |

### Explanatory focus

- Whether teacher learning is produced through participation in a sustained shared practice, not merely collaboration, membership, a digital platform, or scheduled meetings.
- The page should make mutual engagement, joint enterprise, and shared repertoire visible as required evidence.

### Analysis unit

- A teacher community of practice or a bounded participation setting.
- Possible secondary units: newcomer participation, mentoring, co-planning group, subject team, online teacher community, artefact/repertoire, boundary encounter, participation trajectory.

### Data materials

- Observation of recurring participation, co-planning, peer interaction, mentoring, inquiry, or problem-solving episodes.
- Artefacts such as shared tools, lesson plans, routines, language, stories, protocols, platform exchanges, and boundary objects.
- Interviews about belonging, legitimacy, access, recognition, and changes in participation.
- Longitudinal or repeated data if the claim is development over time.
- Programme attendance lists alone are insufficient.

### Strengths

- Creates a strong long-tail page for users unsure whether CoP fits teacher learning data.
- Prevents conceptual overuse of “community of practice.”
- Builds a clear bridge between `communities-of-practice` and `teacher-professional-development-theory` while protecting their boundaries.

### Limitations

- CoP can hide hierarchy, exclusion, coercion, power, or institutional constraints if not explicitly analysed.
- A short-term team, workshop, online forum, or PLC label is not enough evidence.
- CoP is not the best primary route if the research object is individual identity, policy implementation, field inequality, or network resource access.

### Core questions

1. What is the shared practice, and who participates in it?
2. Is there evidence of mutual engagement, joint enterprise, and shared repertoire?
3. How do newcomers, experienced members, or boundary actors gain legitimacy and access?
4. What artefacts, routines, language, or stories show a repertoire rather than a formal programme label?
5. Is the study actually about professional development, identity, social capital, or policy implementation instead?

### Question categories

| Category | Description | Candidate theories |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Participation and practice | Learning through sustained mutual engagement, joint enterprise, and repertoire. | `communities-of-practice` |
| Teacher professional growth | Learning outcomes, changed practice, mediating processes, and development conditions. | `teacher-professional-development-theory` |
| Resource access through ties | Access to information, support, or resources through relations rather than shared practice. | `social-capital-theory` |
| Professional self-understanding | Interpretation of self and work within or beyond participation. | `teacher-identity-theory` |

### Selection path

1. **Test the CoP conditions.** Prompt: What evidence shows mutual engagement, joint enterprise, and shared repertoire? Routing rule: do not use CoP as primary if only formal membership is documented.
2. **Name the learning process.** Prompt: How does participation change knowledge, competence, identity, recognition, or practice? Routing rule: connect participation evidence to a learning claim.
3. **Check duration and access.** Prompt: Is the community sustained enough to study participation trajectories and legitimacy? Routing rule: short events need a different lens unless repeated practice is documented.
4. **Separate alternatives.** Prompt: Is the core mechanism professional growth, self-understanding, resource access, or policy implementation? Routing rule: use TPD, identity, social capital, or SLB when their mechanism is primary.

- Existing topic: `teachers-professional-identity-during-reform` for contrast

### Meta intent
